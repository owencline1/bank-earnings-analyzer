## Divergences (most contested first)

### Notable Q&A pushback
*DIVERGENCE -- expense-guidance pushback (1), reserve-philosophy challenge (1), rotce target skepticism rebuffed (1), m&a pushback / organic-only (1), ndfi loss-risk pushback (1), deflected ndfi/private-credit risk fears (1), m&a/sale-rumor pushback (1). Odd ones out on 'expense-guidance pushback': JPM*

- **JPM** [expense-guidance pushback] Gerard Cassidy pressed on how JPM would cut expenses over the next three quarters to hit the ~$105 billion guide; Barnum pushed back that the firm does not run around finding expenses to cut and that the $105 billion is an outcome of business results, with Dimon adding they would happily spend more if every quarter were this strong.
  > "The $105 billion is not a promise, it's an outcome of business results." - James Dimon, Chairman & CEO
- **BAC** [reserve-philosophy challenge] Saul Martinez (HSBC) pushed back on the reserve release amid macro uncertainty, suggesting BofA reserves less conservatively than peers; Borthwick rejected the framing, attributing lower reserves to a higher-quality loan mix and lowest stress-test loss rates, noting they actually skewed reserves toward the downside/baseline case while releasing only on CRE office improvement.
  > "I was a little bit surprised that you saw the slight reserve release this quarter given the macro uncertainties. And -- so I guess how are you factoring in macro volatility, downside case scenarios into reserving? Because it does seem like you take a different approach to reserving some of your peers." - Saul Martinez, HSBC (analyst)
- **WFC** [ROTCE target skepticism rebuffed] Ebrahim Poonawala of Bank of America pushed back that the 17%-18% ROTCE target looks tough given margin pressure and may have been too aggressive; management firmly defended the path, citing multiple organic growth drivers and confidence the target is achievable.
  > "The path to the 17% to 18% ROTCE is looking quite tough given what is happening with the margin." - Ebrahim Poonawala, Bank of America
- **C** [M&A pushback / organic-only] Mike Mayo pressed Fraser to be unequivocal that Citi is not pursuing any acquisition despite numerous press articles; Fraser flatly reaffirmed the firm is focused solely on organic growth.
  > "I am always transparent. I am always straightforward with you. I want to be crystal clear: we are not interested in anything other than organic growth." - Jane Fraser, CEO
- **USB** [NDFI loss-risk pushback] Gerard Cassidy (RBC) and others pressed management on the NDFI / business credit intermediary portfolio and what scenario would trigger losses; management defended the structures as having very low loss likelihood while acknowledging they 'never say never.'
  > "As bankers, we never say never — that is why we have limits, underwriting practices, and risk management. It is hard to envision the trigger, but rigor and limits are there for that reason." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [deflected NDFI/private-credit risk fears] Chris McGratty (KBW) pressed on where NDFI/private-credit risk ranks in PNC's wall of worry; Demchak forcefully dismissed it, saying it is 'not even on the curve' and contrasting it with real concerns like trucking and fuel-dependent borrowers.
  > "It is not even on the curve. If you go through that whole bucket, the riskiest piece in the whole thing is that little $5 billion slice that is to REITs, leasing, and this and that." - Bill Demchak, CEO
- **TFC** [M&A/sale-rumor pushback] Erika Najarian (UBS) and Mike Mayo (Wells Fargo) pressed Bill Rogers on a Bloomberg article speculating Truist could be a sale/merger target and on succession; Rogers dismissed the rumor and repeatedly stated M&A is not a priority, emphasizing the standalone plan.
  > "We've been really clear on the M&A front, Mike. I mean I don't know how to be more clear that, that's just not a priority for us." - William Rogers, Chairman and CEO

### NIM / NII trajectory & outlook
*DIVERGENCE -- held (2), expanding (2), raised (1), compressing (1), lowered (1). Odd ones out on 'raised': BAC*

- **JPM** [held] Management held NII ex Markets guidance at about $95 billion and lowered total NII to about $103 billion as Markets NII falls to about $8 billion, driven predominantly by lower rates; asset sensitivity offset was small because rate changes were backdated.
  > "We now expect total NII to be approximately $103 billion as a function of market NII decreasing to about $8 billion, predominantly due to rates, which we expect will be primarily offset in NIR." - Jeremy Barnum, CFO
- **BAC** [raised] NII was $15.9B FTE, up 9% YoY with net interest yield of 2.07% (up 8 bps YoY); management raised full-year 2026 NII growth guidance to 6%-8% versus 2025, driven by outperformance, a curve that now assumes no rate cuts, fixed-rate asset repricing and balance sheet growth.
  > "we're raising our full year NII growth guidance range for 2026 versus 2025 to be up 6% to 8%, and that outlook continues to assume moderate deposit and loan growth." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [compressing] NIM fell 13 bps sequentially driven by Markets balance sheet growth, rising interest-bearing deposits, and lower rates; management expects additional but lessening margin compression next quarter and retained full-year NII guidance of ~$50 billion +/-.
  > "You will see a little bit more compression from the first two drivers, but it will be less as we go into the second quarter." - Michael Santomassimo, CFO
- **C** [held] NII ex-Markets was up 7% in Q1, helped partly by FX, and management reaffirmed full-year guidance of approximately 5%-6% growth anchored by mid-single-digit loan and deposit growth driven by client activity.
  > "On NII, what we guided for the year—on the deck—is 5% to 6% NII ex Markets growth, anchored by mid-single-digit growth for both loans and deposits." - Gonzalo Luchetti, CFO
- **USB** [expanding] NIM was flat at 2.77% as core loan growth and stable deposit pricing offset elevated mortgage prepayments and tighter spreads; management expects continued margin progression and reiterated a path to 3%, with full-year NII growth guided to 4%-6%.
  > "Net interest margin was flat linked quarter at 2.77%, as core loan growth and stable deposit pricing were offset by elevated mortgage prepayments and somewhat tighter credit spreads." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [expanding] NIM was 2.95%, up 11 bps sequentially, and management expects to exceed 3% in the second half driven mainly by fixed-rate asset repricing; full-year NII is guided up approximately 14.5%.
  > "Our net interest margin was 2.95%, an increase of 11 basis points." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [lowered] NII fell 2.8% QoQ on two fewer days and seasonal deposit mix; full-year 2026 NII growth guidance was cut to 2%-3% from 3%-4% because the Fed funds rate is now expected to remain unchanged. NIM dipped 5 bps to 3.02% but full-year 2026 average NIM is still expected to exceed 2025's 3.03%.
  > "For full year 2026, we now expect net interest income to increase 2% to 3% compared with our prior expectation of 3% to 4% growth." - Michael Maguire, CFO

### Commercial real estate (CRE) reserves
*DIVERGENCE -- releasing (2), building (1), flat (1). Odd ones out on 'building': JPM*

- **JPM** [building] JPM recorded a net reserve build of $191 million this quarter; weights were left unchanged and a lower weighted-average unemployment assumption created consumer tailwinds, with small builds in wholesale from loan growth and idiosyncratic downgrades. CRE/office was not specifically called out; management consciously decided not to add downside skew despite Middle East risks.
  > "we actually did not change the weights this quarter." - Jeremy Barnum, CFO
- **BAC** [releasing] Provision included a modest net reserve RELEASE driven by improvements in CRE office (and card), as the office portfolio shrank with fewer NPLs and the first quarter in over 3 years with no new NPL inflows into office; this release was partially offset by growth-related builds in corporate/commercial lending.
  > "The only thing that happened to offset that was we had some release from the continued improvement in CRE office where we had built reserve pretty significantly. And as that portfolio has gotten smaller, as we've got less and less in the way of NPLs and [indiscernible] there, we've been able to take some of the reserve back out." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [releasing] Allowance for credit losses rose modestly overall from higher C&I and auto balances, but was partially offset by lower allowance specifically for commercial real estate office (and credit card), indicating a reserve release on office CRE driven by improved/declining exposure there.
  > "A modest increase in our allowance for credit losses for loans was driven by higher commercial and industrial and auto loan balances, largely offset by lower allowance for commercial real estate office and credit card loans." - Michael Santomassimo, CFO
- **C** [not discussed] Citi did not discuss commercial real estate or office CRE specifically; reserve commentary centered on a firm-wide net ACL build of $597 million reflecting a downside-skewed macro scenario, not CRE.
- **USB** [not discussed] Management did not specifically discuss CRE loan-loss reserve builds or releases or office CRE; CRE was only cited as a contributor to broad-based loan growth, and the overall allowance was held at nearly $8 billion (2% of loans).
- **PNC** [flat] PNC said it had already reserved heavily for office CRE and is 'through it', and CRE balances reached an inflection point with growth of roughly $100 million; the total allowance was $5.5 billion or 1.52% of loans, with provision driven by loan growth rather than a CRE build.
  > "Remember when we highlighted our real estate book—we said we were worried about office, we are through it, we reserved a lot of it." - Bill Demchak, CEO
- **TFC** [not discussed] Management did not disclose a specific CRE reserve build, release, or flat action this quarter; CRE was mentioned only as a contributor to wholesale loan growth and as a portfolio that improved within nonperforming loans. No explicit office CRE reserve commentary was given.

### Deposit costs / betas / mix
*DIVERGENCE -- falling (3), stable (3), rising (1). Odd ones out on 'rising': WFC*

- **JPM** [falling] Deposit competition remains intense; interest-bearing deposit costs fell with the rate curve, and management expects the margin to stay roughly the same with limited room to price down the already-low-cost low-beta portion of the franchise.
  > "The margin would be about what it is today, give or take, a couple of basis points up or down." - James Dimon, Chairman & CEO
- **BAC** [falling] Total rate paid on deposits fell 16 bps to 1.47%, aided by a high-quality, low-cost mix and discipline that lets BofA avoid chasing CDs; with no rate cuts assumed, management sees little impetus to change interest-bearing pricing and noted noninterest-bearing deposits picked up.
  > "The total rate paid on our deposits declined 16 basis points to 1.47%, and this allows us to maintain one of the lowest cost funding profiles among the large U.S. banks." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [rising] Deposit mix is shifting toward higher-cost interest-bearing balances (especially commercial) following the asset cap removal, pressuring NII ex-Markets, but management says they are not leaning on price to grow and deposit pricing competition is not unusual.
  > "Those deposits are priced where the market is, which is competitive, but we are not leaning in on price to grow there." - Michael Santomassimo, CFO
- **C** [stable] Management described deposit betas as quite stable, citing pricing discipline and the strength of its value proposition, with deposit growth driven by client activity (Services up 16%, Wealth up 4%).
  > "Pricing discipline helps—betas are quite stable for us, a proof that our value proposition is performing and how embedded we are with clients, our global network on Services, and the quality of our advice and engagement on the Wealth business." - Gonzalo Luchetti, CFO
- **USB** [stable] Deposit pricing was stable with the deposit mix improving as record consumer deposits offset wholesale seasonality; management is reducing CDs and higher-cost institutional deposits and expects to hold the line on costs.
  > "On deposits, yes — we have seen stabilization in deposit mix. We are ultra-focused on growing consumer deposits and, on the wholesale side, on operational deposits. We have been reducing CDs and higher-cost institutional deposits and other less valuable, one-off types." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [stable] Total rate paid on interest-bearing deposits fell 18 bps to 1.96% on lower rates; with no Fed cuts expected, management sees deposit costs as fairly steady, rising perhaps 1-2 bps from back-book repricing rather than competition.
  > "If the Fed does not cut, which is our expectation, deposit costs are fairly steady through the second quarter and then, by our estimates, maybe go up 1 or 2 basis points." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [falling] Average interest-bearing deposit costs fell 14 bps QoQ to 2.09% and total deposit costs fell 9 bps to 1.55%, though management flagged a competitive, yield-seeking environment with cumulative betas ticking up (IB beta 45% to 46%, total 30% to 31%).
  > "Average interest-bearing deposit costs declined 14 basis points linked quarter to 2.09% and average total deposit costs declined 9 basis points to 1.55%." - Michael Maguire, CFO

### Loan growth outlook
*DIVERGENCE -- steady (3), accelerating (3), soft (1). Odd ones out on 'soft': JPM*

- **JPM** [soft] Overall loan growth is modest outside Card (expected ~6% or a little more); this quarter's strong balance-sheet growth was primarily Markets-driven, low-density secured financing, with acquisition financing and data-center lending as opportunistic drivers held on balance sheet.
  > "But to a significant degree, some of that's going to get driven by acquisition financing, that we hold on balance sheet for a while, that some of that's a little bit of a driver this quarter as well." - Jeremy Barnum, CFO
- **BAC** [steady] Average loans grew nearly 9% YoY, led by commercial portfolios, with consumer up ~4% (incl. 3% card); growth was described as broad-based organic core client activity, with the C&I pickup driven mostly by revolver/credit-line utilization (~$5B-$10B) rather than acquisitions.
  > "Average balances grew nearly 9% year-over-year driven primarily by client demand in our commercial portfolios. That growth was broad-based, and it reflects good core operating client activity." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [accelerating] Period-end loans grew 11% year over year, topping $1 trillion, driven by organic growth across commercial and consumer portfolios; management said full-year average loan growth could exceed the prior mid-single-digit assumption if demand holds, and is focused on organic rather than acquisition-driven growth.
  > "Average loans grew 4% in the first quarter from the beginning of the year, and if demand remains strong, average loan growth could be higher than the mid-single-digit increase we had previously assumed." - Michael Santomassimo, CFO
- **C** [steady] Loans ex-Markets grew about 5% in line with guidance, driven by client activity in Wealth, U.S. Cards and Services; management was emphatic that growth is purely organic and that no acquisitions are being pursued.
  > "In terms of loans, ex Markets, we are growing at about 5%, which is in line with our guidance. You can see that coming through in Wealth, in U.S. Cards, and also in Services with export financing and working capital. Most of the growth is driven by client activity." - Gonzalo Luchetti, CFO
- **USB** [accelerating] Average loans grew 3.8% YoY (5.3% ex-2025 loan sales) with broad-based organic strength in commercial, credit card, and CRE; management raised its full-year outlook to mid-single digits, plus an incremental ~$1.6B from the Amazon partnership in Q3.
  > "We have talked about loan growth being in that 3% to 4% range, but I certainly think it is going to be higher than that — probably more in the mid-single-digit range — for the full year." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [accelerating] Average loans grew $23 billion or 7% sequentially, a three-year high in organic growth, with $15 billion from the FirstBank acquisition and $14 billion of legacy organic growth; full-year loans are guided up ~11% but back-half growth is expected to moderate from Q1/Q2 levels.
  > "On a spot basis, loans increased $29 billion, or 9% from year-end, including $15 billion from the FirstBank acquisition and $14 billion of growth in legacy The PNC Financial Services Group, Inc. loans." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [steady] Average loans rose 0.7% QoQ to $327 billion, driven by 1.8% commercial growth offset by a 0.9% consumer decline; management reaffirmed organic 3%-4% average loan growth for 2026 and stated M&A is not a priority, so growth is organic.
  > "Based on our current pipeline and economic outlook, we continue to expect average loan growth of approximately 3% to 4% in 2026." - Michael Maguire, CFO

### Macro / consumer-health view
*DIVERGENCE -- cautious (3), constructive (3), balanced (1). Odd ones out on 'balanced': TFC*

- **JPM** [cautious] Management views the consumer and economy as resilient but cautions that if a credit cycle comes it will be worse than people expect, with stress on leveraged companies refinancing; not deemed systemic but management is preparing for recession/stagflation.
  > "if there's a credit cycle, I do expect it will be worse than people think relative to the scenario. It's not a disaster. We're used to the credit cycles. We'll be big boys about it. But asset prices will go down, credit spreads will going down. People may get a little nervous about some of those things." - James Dimon, Chairman & CEO
- **BAC** [constructive] Management sees a resilient economy with U.S. GDP growth around 2%, a strong, broadly spending consumer supported by solid wage growth and low unemployment, while acknowledging risks (Middle East, trade, inflation); overall the macro backdrop is characterized as constructive.
  > "In our view, while those risks are out there, the macro backdrop remains constructive and a diversified business model position us well to continue to deliver for you across a range of economic scenarios." - Brian Moynihan, Chair and CEO
- **WFC** [cautious] Management sees a resilient but bifurcated consumer with lower-income households more exposed to higher rates and energy prices, an orderly cooling labor market, and corporate clients approaching the year cautiously; they expect some economic impact from energy prices but feel good about where customers stand.
  > "it is likely there will be some economic impact based on what has already occurred, but there are both risks and potential mitigants, so it is hard to predict the ultimate impact." - Charles Scharf, CEO
- **C** [cautious] Management was cautious on the macro backdrop, citing Middle East conflict impacts, rising inflation risk and potentially more restrictive monetary policy, and reflected this by skewing reserves further to a downside scenario with ~7% unemployment.
  > "And inflation is now a greater risk to growth and will likely cause central banks to lean towards more restrictive monetary policies." - Jane Fraser, CEO
- **USB** [constructive] Management views the macro backdrop as constructive despite some softening sentiment, citing stable consumer spend, core loan demand, and delinquency trends, with sentiment shifting toward healthy core middle-market demand.
  > "As we look ahead, the macroeconomic backdrop remains constructive, despite some softening of sentiment recently. Consumer spend, core loan demand, and credit delinquency trends all indicate relative stability." - Gunjan Kedia, President and CEO
- **PNC** [constructive] Management's base case assumes ~1.9% GDP growth, unemployment drifting to 4.6%, and no Fed rate cuts in 2026; they noted weak confidence surveys but said actual client activity and spending are at odds with that, with spending accelerating.
  > "When you look through spending patterns, growth in savings, activity levels, loan growth—what we see day to day in our business is almost at complete odds with the surveys you see on confidence." - Bill Demchak, CEO
- **TFC** [balanced] Management framed the environment as competitive with higher-for-longer rates (no Fed cuts now assumed in 2026), citing net in-migration into its markets as a tailwind while acknowledging deposit-gathering pressure; overall tone was constructive on momentum but cautious on rates.
  > "if rates higher for longer is a tough operating environment for deposits" - William Rogers, Chairman and CEO

### Credit normalization
*DIVERGENCE -- stable (6), improving (1). Odd ones out on 'improving': C*

- **JPM** [stable] Consumer credit metrics look consistent with prior trends and fundamentally healthy, with the card NCO rate outlook held at approximately 3.4%; management attributes resilience mainly to a strong labor market.
  > "Early roll rates, delinquency rates, cash buffer, spend, discretionary spend, non-discretionary spend, it all looks consistent with prior trends and fundamentally, healthy." - Jeremy Barnum, CFO
- **BAC** [stable] Credit performance was described as stable and benign, with net charge-offs of ~$1.4B and a 48 bps net loss rate, down YoY and modestly up sequentially on card seasonality; net charge-offs, card delinquencies, reservable criticized assets and nonperforming loans all declined versus Q1 2025.
  > "Net charge-offs were approximately $1.4 billion with a net loss rate of 48 basis points. Both of those were down from Q1 '25 and modestly up from Q4, primarily reflecting the normal seasonality in our card portfolio with continued stability across the commercial portfolio and improved results in CRE office loans." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [stable] Net charge-off ratio was stable at 45 bps year over year with consumer NCOs down 8 bps and improved early-stage delinquencies; commercial NCOs rose modestly to 24 bps, partly from an isolated fraud loss, and management sees no systemic weakness.
  > "We continue to closely monitor our portfolios for signs of weakness but have not observed recent deterioration or meaningful shifts in trends." - Michael Santomassimo, CFO
- **C** [improving] Card credit performance improved with net credit losses down 11% and delinquencies declining in a prime-weighted portfolio, while corporate net credit losses and nonaccruals remained low; management guided U.S. Cards NCL rate to 4%-4.5%.
  > "With our portfolio heavily weighted to prime, delinquencies and credit losses declined and are well in line with expectations." - Jane Fraser, CEO
- **USB** [stable] Credit quality remained stable with nonperforming assets improving to 0.38%; the net charge-off ratio ticked up 2 bps to 0.56% driven by normal credit card seasonality, with management characterizing client resilience as strong.
  > "The first quarter net charge-off ratio was 0.56%, increasing 2 basis points sequentially, driven by the seasonal nature of credit cards, while our allowance for credit losses of nearly $8 billion represented 2% of period-end loans." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [stable] Credit quality remained strong with NPL and delinquency ratios improving both sequentially and year over year; NPLs were 0.62% of loans, down from 0.67%, and the linked-quarter balance growth was entirely from FirstBank.
  > "Our NPL and delinquency ratios each improved on both a linked-quarter and year-over-year basis, reflecting the strong credit quality we continue to see across our portfolio, and the linked-quarter growth in balances was entirely attributable to the addition of FirstBank." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [stable] Asset quality remained strong; net charge-offs rose 4 bps QoQ to 61 bps and nonperforming loans rose 2 bps to 50 bps, with the consumer NPL increase driven by a change in indirect auto nonaccrual criteria rather than credit deterioration. Full-year NCO guidance unchanged at about 55 bps.
  > "The increase in consumer nonperforming loans was primarily due to a change in the nonaccrual criteria for certain indirect auto loans, which we disclosed in our 10-K rather than any deterioration in underlying credit trends." - Michael Maguire, CFO

### Capital return & regulation
*DIVERGENCE -- increasing (6), steady (1). Odd ones out on 'steady': JPM*

- **JPM** [steady] CET1 was 14.3% with about $40 billion of excess capital; management prefers to deploy capital serving clients over buybacks and would rather repurchase shares only at a real discount, while warning Basel III endgame and G-SIB reproposals would raise JPM's required capital.
  > "Our preferred way of using capital is not buying back stock today. We're doing it, fair market value and all that, but I'd rather buy back stock when we think it's a real discount, and the ongoing shareholder gets the benefit of buying it cheap." - James Dimon, Chairman & CEO
- **BAC** [increasing] BofA returned $2B in dividends and $7.2B in buybacks, letting CET1 drift down 14 bps to 11.2% as capital return outpaced earnings; with growing clarity on Basel III Endgame and G-SIB indexing expected to net to lower requirements, management is moving toward a tighter ~50 bps buffer over minimums.
  > "This quarter, we paid $2 billion in common dividends and we bought back $7.2 billion of common shares." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [increasing] Wells returned $5.4 billion to shareholders including $4 billion of buybacks, ended at CET1 of 10.3% with significant excess capital, and views proposed Basel III rules as constructive with RWAs potentially down ~7% and G-SIB staying around 1.5%.
  > "And finally, we returned 5.4 billion dollars to shareholders in the first quarter, including 4 billion dollars in common stock repurchases, while continuing to operate with significant excess capital." - Charles Scharf, CEO
- **C** [increasing] Citi executed a record $6.3 billion in buybacks, nearing completion of its $20 billion plan, ended at 12.7% CET1, and signaled more capital return to come with details at Investor Day while holding a 100-110bp management buffer.
  > "another piece goes into the buybacks that we executed in the quarter, which are a high watermark at $6.3 billion. There will be more to come as we go into Investor Day." - Gonzalo Luchetti, CFO
- **USB** [increasing] CET1 was 10.8% (9.3% incl. AOCI); buybacks were doubled from $100M to $200M this quarter with further glide-up anticipated, and management reaffirmed a 70%-75% payout target while citing expected RWA relief from Basel III proposals as supportive of returning to historical deployment.
  > "The dividend is extremely important, and buybacks — we went from $100 million to $200 million this quarter. I would anticipate we will continue to glide up." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [increasing] PNC returned $1.4 billion to shareholders ($700 million each dividends and buybacks), expects quarterly repurchases of $600-700 million, and anticipates a ~10% RWA reduction (worth close to a point of capital) under Basel III, with plans to return more capital than in prior years.
  > "During the quarter, we returned $1.4 billion of capital to shareholders. Common dividends and share repurchases were approximately $700 million each, and we continue to expect quarterly repurchases to be in the range of $600 million to $700 million going forward." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [increasing] Truist raised its full-year 2026 buyback target to roughly $5 billion from $4 billion (after $1.1 billion repurchased in Q1, targeting $1.2 billion in Q2), with CET1 stable at 10.8%; the increase was driven by capital planning rather than Basel III, which could cut RWAs 9%-11%.
  > "We are targeting repurchases of $1.2 billion in the second quarter and approximately $5 billion in 2026, compared with our previous expectation for $4 billion of repurchases for full year 2026." - Michael Maguire, CFO

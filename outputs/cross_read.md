## Divergences (most contested first)

### Notable Q&A pushback
*DIVERGENCE -- defensive-pushback (1), defensive-clarifying (1), defensive-but-measured (1), defensive-confident (1), defensive-constructive (1). Odd ones out on 'defensive-pushback': JPM*

- **JPM** [defensive-pushback] Mike Mayo pressed management on why buybacks make sense at ~3x tangible book when the environment could deteriorate, and whether negative operating leverage was acceptable. Dimon pushed back sharply, rejecting the framing of buybacks as 'returning money to shareholders,' explaining it is an investment decision, and that with global capital deployment opportunities (infrastructure, AI, Europe, organic growth) the firm would rather deploy excess capital productively. He also flatly dismissed perpetual operating leverage as a 'crazy notion' for a firm already generating exceptional returns.
  > "buying back stock is not returning money to shareholders. You're making an investment decision, you're not making a return money to shareholders decisions." - Jamie Dimon, Chairman and CEO
- **BAC** [defensive-clarifying] Mike Mayo (Wells Fargo) pressed management repeatedly and pointedly on whether the NII guide was being sandbagged — noting investor feedback that the guide 'is conservative' — and pushed on operating leverage sustainability and commercial loan growth composition. Alistair acknowledged tougher H2 comps and guided to ~8% full-year NII growth, walking back from the more bullish framing by clarifying the comp dynamic rather than raising guidance further. The exchange also surfaced the candid admission that the full-year NII number 'looks more like 8%' even with upper-end range language.
  > "So the investor feedback so far is that they feel that the net interest income guide is conservative. So maybe I'll just re-unpack the number of questions that you've gotten already on this, Alistair." - Mike Mayo (Equity Analyst, Wells Fargo)
- **WFC** [not discussed] No live Q&A transcript was available — the source document is the written earnings release (8-K Ex. 99.1), not the earnings call transcript. Therefore no analyst Q&A exchanges can be reported.
- **C** [not discussed] This document is a written earnings release (8-K Ex. 99.1), not a call transcript; no Q&A session is included or available for analysis. The source explicitly notes the call transcript was not freely published on any parsable source as of the filing date.
- **USB** [defensive-but-measured] Ken Usden (Autonomous Research) pressed management on whether the $98M June BTIG revenue was a sustainable run rate given that only $200M/quarter is embedded in forward guidance — implying a significant step-down. John Stern acknowledged the record month reflected unusual capital markets activity, cited seasonality, and defended the 200M/quarter guide while admitting it 'could go higher,' but maintained that the 15% contribution margin (net of $60M of integration costs) is the right core base for now.
  > "200 per quarter is what we anticipate. You know, I think there's some seasonality, you know, in the third and fourth, you know, they had a record quarter, record month in June. You know, a lot of, a lot of transactions. Could it go higher than 200? It could, but you know, capital markets, you know, fees can swing and things of that nature." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [defensive-confident] Erika Najarian (UBS) pressed management on why the stock was down despite a beat-and-raise quarter, and challenged them on why investors should focus on NIM at all if NII dollars and EPS are growing. Bill Demchak pushed back directly, arguing NIM is the wrong metric to focus on, that PNC would always choose higher EPS over higher NIM, and that the mechanical path above 3% NIM by year-end remains intact regardless.
  > "Yeah, so again, we don't give NIM guidance nor do we manage to it. That said, I always give NIM guidance. So we're above 3. Erica. The precise level at the exit run rate. Why do you care? Is it's, you know, at the end of the day we'll, we'll stick to our guide and we'll get there. But, but if we grow EPS and NII, you know, at 2% higher and have a lower NIM or which, you know, to Rob's earlier point, why, why do you focus on it? So I, I personally don't care." - William Demchak, Chairman Of The Board
- **TFC** [defensive-constructive] Mike Mayo of Wells Fargo pressed Bill Rogers directly on the stock's decade-long underperformance, calling it 'dead money' while peer banks and the S&P nearly doubled, and asked what could be done better going forward. Rogers acknowledged the shared objective of building a high-performing company, pointed to technology investments, talent, and strategic clarity as the path forward, and framed the incoming CEO Mike Lyons as an accelerant — but declined to relitigate the past or offer specific new commitments.
  > "Hey, Bill, from the other comments, I do think you're a great human being, but as you know, I've been extremely disappointed about the results this decade where the stock has been kind of dead money when the stock banks are up almost half and the S&P is almost double. So I've been very frustrated over time." - Mike Mayo, Analyst at Wells Fargo Securities

### Deposit costs / betas / mix
*DIVERGENCE -- peaking (2), falling (2), rising (2), stable (1). Odd ones out on 'stable': C*

- **JPM** [peaking] Management noted that consumer deposit betas have outperformed (i.e., come in better than modeled), meaning deposit costs have risen less than expected. Yield-seeking flows remain a factor and a modest risk in a higher-for-longer rate environment, but overall deposit cost dynamics are in line with or better than prior expectations. Management flagged convexity risk if rates move meaningfully higher but said that inflection point has not yet arrived.
  > "our actual sensitivity is a little bit more than the ear suggests right now because of the outperformance of the consumer betas relative to the model and that difference is probably disproportionately in the front end." - Jeremy Barnum, Chief Financial Officer
- **BAC** [falling] Deposit costs moved modestly lower this quarter, led by consumer deposits with a rate paid of 48bp on $957B in balances. Management emphasized that the favorable mix — including seven consecutive quarters of non-interest-bearing deposit growth (up 4% YoY) — is the key driver of competitive funding costs, not rate-cutting aggression. BAC's strategy focuses on relationship-value deposits (operating accounts, checking) rather than chasing rate-sensitive balances.
  > "As shown in the upper right, rate paid was modestly lower this quarter led by consumer deposits of 48 basis points on 957 billion in balances, so favorable balance moves." - Alistair (CFO)
- **WFC** [falling] Deposit costs are falling and acting as a tailwind to NII. Lower deposit pricing was cited as a primary driver of NII growth year-over-year in multiple segments, including Wealth and Investment Management where NII rose 17% driven explicitly by 'lower deposit pricing.' The release does not provide a specific deposit cost rate or beta figure.
  > "Net interest income was up 17% driven by lower deposit pricing and higher deposit and loan balances" - Earnings Release (Wealth and Investment Management segment)
- **C** [stable] Deposit costs are not explicitly discussed in terms of betas or rate levels. Services NII growth was driven by higher average deposit balances and deposit spreads, while Securities Services NII grew on higher balances but was partially offset by lower deposit spreads — suggesting mixed deposit spread dynamics across segments rather than a uniform directional trend.
  > "The increase in net interest income was driven by higher average deposit balances and deposit spreads." - Earnings Release
- **USB** [rising] Deposit costs edged up a couple of basis points in Q2, driven by seasonal commercial outflows offset by record consumer deposit growth. Management signaled costs could rise further if loan growth remains strong, but characterized the competitive environment as largely unchanged and described deposit pricing strategy as steady and targeted.
  > "You know, that rate is going to be dependent on just how strong loan growth is. I think the stronger the loan growth is, potential rates might go up on that deposit side." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [peaking] Total rate paid on interest-bearing deposits fell 5 bps linked quarter to 1.91%, with declines across all deposit categories. NIB balances grew 4% LQ to 23% of total deposits. Management guided rate paid to drift back up to Q1 levels in coming quarters due to back-book repricing, but characterized the increase as 'not dramatic,' particularly for banks with strong retail franchises.
  > "Our total rate paid on interest-bearing deposits decreased 5 basis points to 1.91% in the second quarter, reflecting lower rates paid across all deposit categories. Notably, average non-interest-bearing balances grew 4% linked quarter and represented 23% of total deposits." - Rob Reilly (Executive Vice President and CFO)
- **TFC** [rising] Deposit costs rose modestly: average interest-bearing deposit costs increased 1 bp linked quarter to 2.10% and total deposit costs rose 1 bp to 1.56%. The cumulative interest-bearing beta fell slightly to 45% from 46%. The bigger concern flagged by management is mix shift — DDA balances are expected to remix down from ~27% to ~25% of deposits by year-end as clients migrate into higher-rate products, which was cited as the single largest headwind to the revised NII outlook.
  > "We continue to see healthy client deposit activity, however, deposit mix trends are being pressured by elevated rate-seeking behavior and migration into higher rate products. Average interest-bearing deposit costs increased by 1 basis point linked quarter to 2.10% and average total deposit cost increased 1 basis point to 1.56%." - Michael Maguire, Chief Financial Officer

### NIM / NII trajectory & outlook
*DIVERGENCE -- raised (3), expanding (3), lowered (1). Odd ones out on 'lowered': TFC*

- **JPM** [raised] JPM raised its full-year 2026 NII X markets guide from $95B to ~$96.5B and total NII to ~$105.5B, with markets NII guided higher to ~$9B. The primary driver of the upward revision was stronger deposit balances (both wholesale and consumer) and a favorable mix shift, with higher short- and long-end rates providing a secondary boost. Management also noted consumer deposit betas have outperformed models, adding incremental sensitivity.
  > "just mechanically in terms of the drivers of the upward revision, the biggest single factor is deposit balances, I would say across both wholesale and consumer both the overall quantum of it, but also makes shift inside of that in favor of slightly higher margin overall then rates are a little bit higher than when we previously guided both in the short end and in the back end." - Jeremy Barnum, Chief Financial Officer
- **BAC** [raised] NII rose to ~$16.2B, up $253M from Q1 and up 9% YoY, driven by loan/deposit growth, fixed-rate asset repricing, and global markets activity. Management raised full-year NII guidance twice during the year — from 5-7% to 6-8% in April, and now to the upper end of the 6-8% range — supported by continued loan/deposit growth and one expected 25bp rate hike in September. Net interest yield was 2.08%, up 1bp QoQ and 14bp YoY, with a longer-term target of ~2.30%.
  > "We now expect full year 2026 NII growth to be at the upper end of that 6 to 8% range supported by anticipated loan and deposit growth, fixed rate asset repricing and balance sheet optimisation and this assumes modest loan and deposit growth in the second half of the year and it's based on the current forward curve which has one 25 basis point rate hike in September." - Alistair (CFO)
- **WFC** [expanding] NII rose 5% year-over-year to $12.3B and 2% sequentially to $12.3B, driven by lower deposit costs, higher loan and investment securities balances, and balance sheet growth in Markets. NIM compressed to 2.43% from 2.68% a year ago and 2.47% last quarter, reflecting the drag from lower rates on floating-rate assets. No explicit full-year NII guidance range was stated in the release.
  > "Net interest income increased 5%, driven by lower deposit costs, higher loan and investment securities balances, the impact of lower interest rates and balance sheet growth in our Markets business, and higher interest-bearing commercial deposits, partially offset by the impact of lower interest rates on floating rate assets, as well as a modest decline in noninterest-bearing deposits" - Earnings Release (Selected Company-wide Financial Information)
- **C** [expanding] Net interest income grew 13% year-over-year, driven by growth across all five businesses and Legacy Franchises, partially offset by a decline in Corporate/Other. The Corporate/Other NII decline was attributed to actions taken to reduce asset sensitivity in a lower interest rate environment, suggesting NII guidance is directionally held-to-expanding at the segment level even as Corporate Treasury positioning weighs on the consolidated figure.
  > "Net interest income increased 13%, driven by growth across each of Citi's five businesses and Legacy Franchises, partially offset by a decline in Corporate/Other." - Earnings Release
- **USB** [expanding] NII on an FTE basis hit $4.4B, up 7.5% YoY and 2.2% sequentially, driven by loan growth, investment portfolio repositioning, and fixed-asset repricing. NIM expanded 2 bps sequentially to 2.79%, with management guiding full-year NII growth to 'north of 5%' and maintaining a path toward 3% NIM sometime in 2027.
  > "We do expect that to grow over the course of the year and that's reflected in the guide and you know, you know, the deposits, you know, we, you know, we think that that's, that nothing's really changed on that front from a competitive nature standpoint." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [raised] NII grew 4% linked quarter to $4.1 billion, driven by commercial loan growth and higher NIB deposit balances. NIM ticked up 1 bp to 2.96%; management reiterated their commitment to crossing 3% by year-end, with full-year NII guided up 15–15.5% vs. 2025, and Q3 NII guided up 3–3.5% sequentially. Higher-credit-quality, lower-spread commercial loans are constraining near-term NIM even as they are highly EPS-accretive.
  > "We expect full-year net interest income to be up 15 to 15.5%" - Rob Reilly (Executive Vice President and CFO)
- **TFC** [lowered] NIM fell 4 bps linked quarter to 2.98%, driven by higher deposit costs, lower loan spreads, and a larger balance sheet. Full-year NII guidance was cut to +1–1.5% from the prior +2–3%, driven by three headwinds: portfolio optimization reducing lower-return consumer loans, broad-based loan spread compression (now expected -5 to -10 bps year-over-year), and an unfavorable deposit mix shift toward higher-rate products; partially offset by higher medium/long-term rates.
  > "We now expect net interest income to increase approximately 1 to 1.5%. Our updated outlook reflects actions we have taken to improve profitability as well as certain market dynamics. First, we are continuing to optimize less strategic and lower return lending portfolios that offer limited relationship potential which has the effect of reducing NII and net interest margin but improves ROTCE." - Michael Maguire, Chief Financial Officer

### Loan growth outlook
*DIVERGENCE -- accelerating (4), steady (2), soft (1). Odd ones out on 'soft': TFC*

- **JPM** [steady] Loan growth was organic and broad-based: AWM cited higher loan balances, CCB highlighted deposit and loan balance growth, and wholesale deposit growth was described as strong and ahead of expectations partly because lending is creating deposits (especially in the MDFI/data center space). Management explicitly framed organic growth as the primary capital deployment priority across every business line. No acquisition-driven loan growth was cited.
  > "some of it is also the kind of lending environment, particularly the sort of MDFI space and a lot of the data center stuff like however you look at it, you have a little bit of the dynamic of loans creating deposits and that's going to disproportionately show up in wholesale." - Jeremy Barnum, Chief Financial Officer
- **BAC** [accelerating] Loan growth was entirely organic — management explicitly highlighted this as a key differentiator. Average loans rose to $1.2T, up $88B or 8% YoY, marking the ninth consecutive quarter of average and ending loan growth. Commercial loans led at $733B, up $75B or 11% YoY, with broad-based growth across business banking, commercial bank, and corporate bank. Consumer loans grew 3% YoY, with card up 4% and securities-based lending strong. No acquisition-driven growth was mentioned.
  > "Average loans and leases increased to 1.2 trillion, up 88 billion or 8% from a year ago. Ending loans were also 1.22 trillion, up $71 billion or 6%, marking the ninth consecutive quarter of both average and ending loan growth." - Alistair (CFO)
- **WFC** [accelerating] Average loans grew 12% year-over-year and 3% quarter-over-quarter to $1,026.5B — all organic, with no acquisitions mentioned as a driver. Growth was broad-based: CIB loans up 26% YoY (aided by Markets balance sheet growth), Commercial Banking up 5%, Consumer up 6%, and WIM up 12%. Management noted deliberate, selective deployment of capital now that the asset cap is no longer a constraint.
  > "Our balance sheet continued to grow with average loans increasing 12% from a year ago, with higher balances in both our consumer and commercial businesses, and average deposits increasing 10%." - Charlie Scharf, Chairman and CEO
- **C** [accelerating] End-of-period loans grew 9% YoY and 4% QoQ to $794B, driven organically by Markets, USCC, and Services. USCC also onboarded the American Airlines co-branded card portfolio in Q2 2026, which is an acquisition-driven contributor to that segment's loan growth. Banking average loans grew 5% YoY, with growth in episodic IB-related loans offsetting declines in core corporate lending balances.
  > "Citigroup's end-of-period loans were $794 billion at quarter end, up 9% versus the prior-year period, primarily driven by higher loans in Markets, USCC and Services." - Earnings Release
- **USB** [accelerating] Average loans grew 7.1% year-over-year and 3.0% sequentially to $405B, with broad-based organic strength in C&I, credit card, and commercial real estate. Inorganic contributions include the BTIG acquisition (primarily fee-based, minimal loan impact) and the pending Amazon Small Business portfolio (~$1.6B in card balances) expected to close mid-August. Management raised the full-year loan growth outlook above prior 'mid-single-digit' guidance.
  > "Average loans totaled $405 billion, up 7.1% from the prior year quarter and 3.0% from the prior quarter. Growth was broad-based in strategic categories such as CNI, credit card, and commercial real estate, which brings ancillary fees with them." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [steady] Total average loans grew $12 billion or 4% linked quarter to $363 billion, virtually all organic and concentrated in C&I (broad-based across categories and geographies, with higher line utilization). CRE grew $690 million; consumer declined $730 million. Full-year average loan growth was guided to ~12.5%; second-half growth is expected to moderate to roughly GDP pace (~1-2% per quarter). The First Bank acquisition was completed but its incremental contribution was not separately quantified for organic vs. acquisition-driven loan growth in Q2.
  > "Virtually all of the loan growth was in CNI, reflecting strong new production and higher utilization across almost every loan category. CRE balances increased $690 million during the quarter, driven primarily by growth in retail and industrial exposures, and consumer loans declined by $730 million as growth in credit card balances partially offset expected declines in residential, real estate, and auto loans." - Rob Reilly (Executive Vice President and CFO)
- **TFC** [soft] All loan growth discussed is organic — no acquisitions were referenced. Average loans grew 0.7% linked quarter to $329B, driven by 1.3% growth in commercial loans (C&I up ~8% year-over-year) while consumer loans declined. Management is deliberately running off less profitable consumer portfolios (marine/RV exited, prime and non-prime auto production cut ~40%), reducing annual production by ~$7–8B. Full-year loan growth is expected in the low single-digit range, concentrated in C&I; consumer will be roughly flat to +1%.
  > "Average loans held for investment increased $2.1 billion or 0.7% linked quarter to $329 billion driven by 1.3% growth in average commercial loans partially offset by a decline in average consumer loans. End of period loans increased modestly linked quarter reflecting slight growth in both commercial and consumer." - Michael Maguire, Chief Financial Officer

### Credit normalization
*DIVERGENCE -- improving (5), stable (1), normalizing (1). Odd ones out on 'stable': BAC*

- **JPM** [improving] Credit performance came in better than expected: JPM reported $2.4B in net charge-offs with a $149M reserve build, and revised its full-year card NCO rate guide down to ~3.2%. Delinquencies are running a little lower than expected across FICO cohorts, and management attributed the resilience squarely to a still-solid labor market.
  > "we now expect the card net charge-off rate to be approximately 3.2% reflecting better than expected consumer credit performance." - Jeremy Barnum, Chief Financial Officer
- **BAC** [stable] Credit quality is described as stable and consistent, with provision expense and net charge-offs both approximately $1.4B and largely unchanged from Q1. Consumer card charge-offs and delinquencies improved both YoY and QoQ. Reservable criticized commercial exposures declined ~$2.3B from Q1, driven primarily by CRE improvement. Management expressed confidence that credit costs have 'flattened out at very strong historical levels.'
  > "Consumer card charge offs and delinquencies improved both year over year and quarter over quarter. Commercial credit also remained solid with CRE improvement offset by some isolated corporate and commercial lending losses. Reservable criticized commercial exposures declined by approximately $2.3 billion from Q1 to roughly 22 billion, driven primarily by CRE improvement." - Alistair (CFO)
- **WFC** [improving] Credit is clearly improving: the NCO rate fell to 0.34% annualized in Q2 2026 from 0.45% in Q1 2026 and 0.44% a year ago. Nonperforming assets dropped $824M quarter-over-quarter. Both consumer and commercial charge-off rates declined, led by lower auto, credit card, and C&I NCOs. Management described consumer and commercial credit quality as 'strong across all portfolios.'
  > "Consumer and commercial credit quality remained strong across all portfolios and net loan charge-offs declined 10 basis points from a year ago." - Charlie Scharf, Chairman and CEO
- **C** [normalizing] Net credit losses rose 8% year-over-year to $2.4B, but the overall provision fell 12% YoY as ACL builds moderated sharply. The reserve-to-funded-loans ratio edged down to 2.5% from 2.7%, and management cited 'better credit performance than expected' in U.S. Consumer Cards — suggesting credit is normalizing/improving rather than deteriorating, with USCC leading the improvement.
  > "Despite short-term headwinds from investments in our U.S. Consumer Cards portfolio, our resilient customer base kept fueling underlying drivers: loan growth, higher spend and better credit performance than expected." - Jane Fraser, Chair and CEO
- **USB** [improving] Credit quality continued to improve both sequentially and year-over-year. The NCO rate fell 3 bps sequentially to 0.53%, and the non-performing asset ratio improved 5 bps sequentially and 11 bps year-over-year to 0.33%, reflecting a stable economic backdrop.
  > "Our ratio of non-performing assets to loans and other real estate of 0.33% improved 5 basis points from the previous quarter and 11 basis points from a year ago. The second quarter net charge-off ratio was 0.53%, decreasing 3 basis points sequentially." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [improving] Credit quality improved across every major metric: NPLs fell 10% to 0.55% of loans, total delinquencies declined to 0.39% of loans, and net charge-offs were $226 million at a 25 bp NCO rate. Management guided Q3 NCOs to approximately $225 million, suggesting stable/improving credit with no notable deteriorating pockets beyond isolated sector pressures (healthcare, distilleries, transportation).
  > "Overall credit quality remains strong with improvements in NPLs, delinquencies, and net loan charge-offs. Non-performing loans of $2 billion decreased $216 million or 10%, and represented 0.55% of total loans, down from 0.62% last quarter. Total delinquencies declined $122 million to $1.4 billion and now represent 0.39% of total loans. Total net loan charge-offs were $226 million and our NCO ratio was 25 basis points." - Rob Reilly (Executive Vice President and CFO)
- **TFC** [improving] Credit improved this quarter: net charge-offs fell 11 bps linked quarter to 50 bps annualized, and the allowance for loan losses dipped 2 bps to 1.51% of total loans. Full-year NCO guidance is held at 55 bps. A technical reclassification of indirect auto loans to non-accrual status lifted non-performing loan ratios by 1 bp but management emphasized this does not reflect deteriorating credit.
  > "Net charge-offs declined 11 basis points linked quarter to 50 basis points, reflecting lower losses across most portfolios compared with the second quarter of 2025. Net charge-offs were relatively stable. Our provision for credit losses totaled $395 million, modestly below net charge-offs of $414 million, resulting in a 2 basis point linked quarter decline in allowance for loan losses to 1.51% of total loans." - Michael Maguire, Chief Financial Officer

### Commercial real estate (CRE) reserves
*DIVERGENCE -- releasing (4), flat (1). Odd ones out on 'flat': USB*

- **JPM** [not discussed] CRE reserves were not specifically broken out or discussed in the call. The firm reported a modest net reserve build of $149M at the firm-wide level, but management did not attribute any portion to CRE or office CRE specifically, nor did analysts press on it.
- **BAC** [releasing] BAC recorded a modest reserve RELEASE this quarter. CRE was cited as a positive driver — criticized CRE exposures declined, contributing to the $2.3B drop in total reservable criticized commercial exposures. The reserve release was modest and the overall portfolio is described as well-positioned. Office CRE was not specifically called out as a separate risk item.
  > "Non-performing loans remain stable at approximately $5.8 billion and we recorded a modest reserve release. Overall, our portfolio remains well positioned, supported by strong client fundamentals and disciplined risk management." - Alistair (CFO)
- **WFC** [releasing] Wells Fargo released CRE allowance for credit losses this quarter. The overall allowance increased modestly, driven by higher reserves for credit card and auto loans due to higher balances, but this was 'largely offset by a lower allowance for commercial real estate loans.' CRE nonaccrual loans also declined quarter-over-quarter, contributing to the reserve release.
  > "Provision for credit losses in second quarter 2026 included a modest increase in the allowance for credit losses, reflecting a higher allowance for credit card and auto loans driven by higher loan balances, largely offset by a lower allowance for commercial real estate loans" - Earnings Release (Selected Company-wide Financial Information)
- **C** [not discussed] Commercial real estate is not explicitly broken out as a standalone reserve discussion in this release. The release covers ACL builds and releases at the segment level; no specific CRE or office CRE reserve action is identified or quantified.
- **USB** [flat] The allowance for credit losses held flat at $8B (1.94% of period-end loans) with no meaningful build or release on the existing book. However, management flagged a forward-looking $160M reserve build tied specifically to the pending Amazon Small Business portfolio acquisition expected to close in mid-August — not driven by CRE stress. Office CRE was not separately called out.
  > "We anticipate $160 million reserve build that will occur with the closing that we anticipate to be mid August." - John Stern, CFO
- **PNC** [releasing] PNC released CRE reserves as the office CRE workout continued. Management noted they have 'worked through the commercial real estate office portfolio' with 'still some work to do,' but explicitly confirmed reserve releases. CRE balances inflected to growth in Q1 2026 for the first time after many quarters of declines, driven by retail and industrial, with constructive pipelines forming across multifamily, industrial, and retail.
  > "We've worked through the commercial real estate office portfolio. Still some work to do there. But we did release some reserves as we work through that book." - Rob Reilly (Executive Vice President and CFO)
- **TFC** [releasing] CRE reserves were modestly released this quarter — the overall ALLL declined 2 bps to 1.51% — driven in part by the resolution of several commercial and CRE problem credits and continued improvement in office and multifamily non-performing loans. Management signaled progress in both office and multifamily as drivers of the reserve reduction.
  > "The modest reduction in our ALLL was primarily driven by the resolution of several commercial and commercial real estate problems. Credits during the quarter and continued improvement in sectors like office and multifamily non-performing loans held for investment increased 1 basis point linked quarter to 51 basis points of total loans as higher indirect auto problem loans were partially offset by improvement in the commercial portfolio." - Michael Maguire, Chief Financial Officer

### Macro / consumer-health view
*DIVERGENCE -- balanced (4), constructive (3). Odd ones out on 'constructive': BAC, USB, PNC*

- **JPM** [balanced] Management described the current environment as 'getting close to as good as it gets' — healthy labor market, strong consumer spend, resilient credit — but explicitly cautioned they don't know how long it will last. Dimon pointed to geopolitical risks, elevated global deficits, and a complex, 'exuberant' market.

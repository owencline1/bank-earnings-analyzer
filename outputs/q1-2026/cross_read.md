## Divergences (most contested first)

### Notable Q&A pushback
*DIVERGENCE -- defensive-but-combative (1), defensive-on-reserving (1), constructive-pushback (1), defensive-definitive (1), cautious-but-confident (1), defensive-constructive (1), defensive-on-m&a (1). Odd ones out on 'defensive-but-combative': JPM*

- **JPM** [defensive-but-combative] Steven Chubak (Wolfe Research) pressed management on whether Basel III/G-SIB guidance contemplates mitigating RWA actions and potential SCB relief from removal of double-counting. Barnum was candid that this is not primarily an optimization story, flagged specific irrational outcomes in the proposal (high-yield repo collateral, advised lines), and made a pointed policy argument that the G-SIB surcharge creates a 'significant disincentive' to markets activity and risks undermining U.S. capital-market depth. Dimon piled on with blunt criticism of operational risk RWA as an 'academic exercise' with no real-world basis.
  > "I guess I would just make the point that we've often made publicly that the depth and breadth of U.S. capital markets is a key competitive national advantage. And regulatory capital rules that at the margin discourage a dynamic secondary market in the United States with active participation by banks is, in our view, sort of not great." - Chief Financial Officer — Jeremy Barnum
- **BAC** [defensive-on-reserving] Saul Martinez (HSBC) pressed management on why BAC released reserves in a quarter marked by macro uncertainty, and challenged whether BAC's reserve ratios — which are lower than peers on coverage of charge-offs and delinquencies — reflect an overly optimistic reserving posture. Borthwick defended the approach by pointing to BAC's structurally lower-risk loan portfolio (lower unsecured consumer exposure, more wealth loans) and its 13-of-14-year record as the lowest loss bank in Fed stress tests, while acknowledging a specific upside-scenario haircut of 5% was moved to baseline this quarter.
  > "The only thing that happened to offset that was we had some release from the continued improvement in CRE office where we had built reserve pretty significantly. And as that portfolio has gotten smaller, as we've got less and less in the way of NPLs and [indiscernible] there, we've been able to take some of the reserve back out. So that's all that's going on in this particular quarter." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [constructive-pushback] Ebrahim Poonawala (BofA) pressed management directly on whether the 17%-18% ROTCE target was realistic given ongoing NIM compression and ROA dilution from the growing repo/Markets balance sheet. Both Scharf and Santomassimo pushed back forcefully, detailing specific business-by-business drivers (credit card vintage maturation, wealth fee growth, commercial banker adds, IB ramp) and Scharf stated categorically that nothing has changed in their confidence on the target.
  > "We feel as confident as ever in that target. There is absolutely nothing that has changed. We do not have a business model where points of view like that should change quarter on quarter. The only thing that would create dramatic changes is if we thought we got something very wrong or if there was some huge event that we missed. None of that is the case." - Charles Scharf (CEO)
- **C** [defensive-definitive] Mike Mayo of Wells Fargo pressed Jane Fraser repeatedly and bluntly on whether Citigroup is pursuing an acquisition — referencing 'so many articles' — and demanded a definitive on-record denial. Fraser responded with an unambiguous, categorical rejection, stating Citi is 'not interested in anything other than organic growth,' which she had already framed as the single key takeaway from the call.
  > "I am always transparent. I am always straightforward with you. I want to be crystal clear: we are not interested in anything other than organic growth." - Jane Fraser (CEO)
- **USB** [cautious-but-confident] The most pointed exchange came from Erika Najarian (UBS), who pressed management on whether the Category II AOCI cliff-in transition — and the lack of regulatory clarity on the five-year phase-in — would delay or constrain USB's path to 70%-75% capital payout. Management acknowledged the timing ambiguity directly but argued the impact is only a quarter or two, not strategically material, and reiterated confidence in their preparation for Category II.
  > "We believe regulators' intent is to allow all banks a five-year phase-in on AOCI to take cliff effects away, but we are waiting for clarification. A very good outcome for capital distributions would be a prompt effective date for current Basel III proposals plus a five-year phase-in; in that case we would be well ahead of our capital needs even as a Cat II and would bring forward distributions." - Gunjan Kedia
- **PNC** [defensive-constructive] Mike Mayo (Wells Fargo) pressed management on how PNC is achieving outsized loan growth relative to peers in what appears to be an industry-wide capital-deployment environment — essentially asking whether PNC is competing on price or taking undue risk. Demchak pushed back firmly, attributing growth to new market expansion (expansion markets now >50% of corporate loans, growing 2x faster), specialty lending products, and integrated relationships — not price competition or loosening standards.
  > "That is not our story. We are bringing all these new markets online. We have more shots on goal. We are seeing more opportunities as opposed to trying to rebid the same deal I have been in for 22 years in our local market. That is a big part of it, and that is why when we went through the Southeast, now it is accelerating—BBVA and FirstBank markets." - Bill Demchak, Chairman, President & CEO
- **TFC** [defensive-on-M&A] The most pointed exchange came from Erika Najarian (UBS), who pressed management directly on a Bloomberg article speculating Truist could be an attractive M&A target—either as an acquirer or a seller—given its low market-cap-to-core-deposits ratio. Rogers deflected the merger speculation by calling it a rumor already refuted, and firmly redirected to the bank's organic profitability plan and new 16%-18% long-term ROTCE target as evidence of a standalone strategy. Mike Mayo then pushed further on succession risk and conditions under which Truist would consider a merger of equals, a sale, or an acquisition, receiving a similarly firm dismissal of M&A as a priority.
  > "we've all been at this a long time and to ask me to speculate on some rumor and some article which by the way, has been repeated, I think, pretty solidly by the person identified in that. So let's just like put that aside." - William Rogers (CEO)

### NIM / NII trajectory & outlook
*DIVERGENCE -- held (3), expanding (2), raised (1), lowered (1). Odd ones out on 'raised': BAC*

- **JPM** [held] Management held NII ex-Markets guidance at ~$95B for FY2026, while total NII was guided to ~$103B (with Markets NII revised down to ~$8B due to lower rates, expected to be offset in non-interest revenue). Despite being asset sensitive with a $1.8B EAR, the removal of expected back-half rate cuts had only ~20 bps impact on the full-year average, producing no material upward revision to ex-Markets NII.
  > "We continue to expect NII ex Markets to be about $95 billion. We now expect total NII to be approximately $103 billion as a function of market NII decreasing to about $8 billion, predominantly due to rates, which we expect will be primarily offset in NIR." - Chief Financial Officer — Jeremy Barnum
- **BAC** [raised] NII came in at $15.9B FTE (+9% YoY), beating expectations, with net interest yield of 2.07% (+8 bps YoY). Management raised full-year 2026 NII growth guidance to +6%–8% vs. 2025 (from a prior lower range), citing the rate curve shift from 2 expected cuts to none, continued fixed-rate asset repricing, balance sheet growth, and funding optimization.
  > "Given our outperformance against expectations of NII in Q1 and based on the most recent interest rate curve, which has now shifted from 2 rate cuts expected to having none currently, we're raising our full year NII growth guidance range for 2026 versus 2025 to be up 6% to 8%, and that outlook continues to assume moderate deposit and loan growth." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [held] NII rose 5% YoY but fell $235M (2%) sequentially due to two fewer days and lower rates; NIM compressed 13 bps from Q4, driven by Markets balance sheet growth, higher interest-bearing deposit mix, and rate headwinds. Full-year 2026 NII guidance held at $50B ± with further modest margin compression expected in Q2 before moderating.
  > "We are retaining our guidance of 50 billion dollars, plus or minus, of net interest income this year." - Michael Santomassimo (CFO)
- **C** [held] NII ex-Markets grew 7% YoY in Q1, beating the full-year guidance range of 5%–6%, driven by client-led deposit and loan volume growth across Services and Wealth. Management held guidance unchanged at 5%–6% for the full year, citing macro uncertainty and deliberate conservatism, and noted the American Airlines/Barclays card portfolio acquisition is already embedded in that guidance.
  > "What we guided for the year—on the deck—is 5% to 6% NII ex Markets growth, anchored by mid-single-digit growth for both loans and deposits. We are pleased that Q1 is a good showing against that." - Gonzalo Luchetti (CFO)
- **USB** [expanding] NIM held flat QoQ at 2.77%, as core loan growth and stable deposit pricing were offset by elevated mortgage prepayments and tighter credit spreads on larger-institution loans. Management maintained full-year NII guidance of 4%-6% growth but expressed a bias toward the higher end on fees; Q2 NII growth is guided at 6%-7% YoY, and management still sees a path to 3% NIM over time, aided by the Amazon portfolio coming online in Q3.
  > "Net interest margin was flat linked quarter at 2.77%, as core loan growth and stable deposit pricing were offset by elevated mortgage prepayments and somewhat tighter credit spreads." - John Stern
- **PNC** [expanding] NIM expanded 11 bps QoQ to 2.95%, driven by lower funding costs, commercial loan growth, and the FirstBank acquisition. Management raised full-year NII guidance to +~14.5% YoY and expects NIM to exceed 3% in the second half of 2026, with expansion continuing to be led by fixed-rate asset repricing.
  > "We still expect to go above 3% in the second half. As you pointed out, we are at 2.95%, so if we are going to be above 3% in the second half, you can do the math in between. Most of the expansion is still coming from the fixed-rate asset repricing. That continues to be very strong." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [lowered] NIM fell 5 bps QoQ to 3.02%, and NII dropped 2.8% ($105M) primarily due to two fewer calendar days and seasonal deposit mix shifts. Full-year NII guidance was cut to 2%-3% growth from 3%-4%, driven by the removal of two expected Fed rate cuts; management expects NIM to remain roughly stable in Q2 before expanding in H2, and for full-year 2026 average NIM to exceed the 2025 average of 3.03%, though a 'mid-3-teens' NIM exit rate is no longer expected in 2026.
  > "For full year 2026, we now expect net interest income to increase 2% to 3% compared with our prior expectation of 3% to 4% growth. The change in our outlook is primarily driven by our expectation that the federal funds rate will remain unchanged throughout 2026 compared with our previous expectation for two 25 basis point reductions, one in April and one in July." - Michael Maguire (CFO)

### Deposit costs / betas / mix
*DIVERGENCE -- stable (3), falling (3), rising (1). Odd ones out on 'rising': WFC*

- **JPM** [stable] Management described deposit margins as roughly stable with only modest basis-point variation expected. Deposit competition remains intense both externally and internally from higher-yielding alternatives, but the low-beta portion of the consumer franchise is already priced low. Consumer deposit costs were noted as declining modestly quarter-over-quarter as a function of the rate curve and CD repricing from the prior year.
  > "The margin would be about what it is today, give or take, a couple of basis points up or down. There are a lot of factors in there, like what kind of accounts you're opening, tax refunds and all that kind of stuff. But roughly the same for now." - Chairman & Chief Executive Officer — James Dimon
- **BAC** [falling] Deposit costs fell 16 bps QoQ to 1.47%, driven by disciplined pricing, mix discipline (avoiding rate-chasing on CDs and repo), and a deliberate strategy to let higher-cost institutional CDs and repo run off. Management indicated no strong impetus to raise deposit rates if the Fed stays on hold, and flagged continued growth in both interest-bearing and noninterest-bearing balances.
  > "The total rate paid on our deposits declined 16 basis points to 1.47%, and this allows us to maintain one of the lowest cost funding profiles among the large U.S. banks." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [rising] Deposit costs are rising at the margin as post-asset-cap removal drives faster growth in higher-cost interest-bearing commercial and consumer deposits; mix is shifting away from noninterest-bearing toward interest-bearing, putting pressure on NII ex-Markets. Management is not competing aggressively on price but acknowledges this mix shift is a headwind that will persist through the year.
  > "We have also grown deposits, and as we said when we provided our outlook last quarter, much of the growth was from interest-bearing deposits, particularly in our commercial businesses. As a reminder, when the asset cap was in place, these deposits were limited, and now that it has been lifted, we are successfully growing these deposits. While they are higher cost, they are important to our strategy of deepening relationships with our clients." - Michael Santomassimo (CFO)
- **C** [stable] Deposit betas are described as 'quite stable,' reflecting the strength of Citi's value proposition and deep client embeddedness, particularly in Services. Deposit pricing is not a headwind; mix management and pricing discipline are cited as contributors to NII growth. No explicit discussion of rising or falling deposit costs.
  > "Pricing discipline helps—betas are quite stable for us, a proof that our value proposition is performing and how embedded we are with clients, our global network on Services, and the quality of our advice and engagement on the Wealth business." - Gonzalo Luchetti (CFO)
- **USB** [stable] Deposit costs were described as stable, with management actively reducing higher-cost CDs and institutional deposits while growing lower-cost consumer and operational deposits. No competitive deposit-pricing pressure was cited; management expressed confidence that costs can hold even if the Fed does not cut rates.
  > "On deposits, yes — we have seen stabilization in deposit mix. We are ultra-focused on growing consumer deposits and, on the wholesale side, on operational deposits. We have been reducing CDs and higher-cost institutional deposits and other less valuable, one-off types." - John Stern
- **PNC** [falling] Deposit costs fell 18 bps QoQ to 1.96% on interest-bearing deposits, reflecting the rate environment and a reduction in brokered CDs. Management expects deposit costs to be roughly flat in Q2 and may drift up 1-2 bps thereafter due to back-book repricing, not competitive pressure — consistent with their no-Fed-cut base case.
  > "If the Fed does not cut, which is our expectation, deposit costs are fairly steady through the second quarter and then, by our estimates, maybe go up 1 or 2 basis points. Generally speaking, the pressure up is not from competition but rather repricing back book as things roll—back book customers to a closer-to-market level." - Rob Reilly, CFO ⚠️UNVERIFIED
- **TFC** [falling] Deposit costs fell for the quarter: average interest-bearing deposit costs declined 14 bps QoQ to 2.09% and total deposit cost declined 9 bps to 1.55%. However, management flagged ongoing competitive pressures and yield-seeking behavior among clients as headwinds, and noted that 'rates higher for longer' creates a tough deposit-gathering environment, suggesting further meaningful relief may be limited without rate cuts.
  > "Average interest-bearing deposit costs declined 14 basis points linked quarter to 2.09% and average total deposit costs declined 9 basis points to 1.55%." - Michael Maguire (CFO)

### Commercial real estate (CRE) reserves
*DIVERGENCE -- flat (4), releasing (2), building (1). Odd ones out on 'building': C*

- **JPM** [flat] CRE reserves were not explicitly broken out as a standalone topic. The overall reserve build of $191M was modest; management noted wholesale builds tied to loan growth and idiosyncratic downgrades, a ~$110-150M consumer Home Lending reserve release on an HPI upward revision, and unchanged scenario weights. Management consciously chose NOT to add downside skew to scenario weights this quarter, concluding existing conservative bias was sufficient.
  > "Under the covers, there are some builds in wholesale as a function of loan growth and also some idiosyncratic downgrades here and there, nothing dramatic. But in the place that you would expect to see allowance build, you are seeing some." - Chief Financial Officer — Jeremy Barnum
- **BAC** [releasing] BAC released CRE reserves this quarter, specifically in the office CRE sub-portfolio, as that book has continued to shrink, NPL inflows into office hit zero for the first time in over 3 years, and underlying credit performance improved. The release was partially offset by growth-related reserve builds in corporate and commercial lending.
  > "Provision expense was approximately $1.3 billion, including a modest net reserve release driven by improvements in card and commercial real estate and partially offset by growth-related and targeted build supporting corporate and commercial lending portfolios." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [releasing] CRE office reserves were RELEASED in Q1 2026; the overall allowance for credit losses rose modestly, driven by higher commercial & industrial and auto loan balances, but this increase was partially offset by a reduction specifically in the CRE office and credit card reserve buckets. Management indicated losses in most of those portfolios have been minimal for a long time and the allowance is low and not changing materially.
  > "A modest increase in our allowance for credit losses for loans was driven by higher commercial and industrial and auto loan balances, largely offset by lower allowance for commercial real estate office and credit card loans." - Michael Santomassimo (CFO)
- **C** [building] CRE was not specifically called out as a distinct reserve driver. The firm reported a firm-wide net ACL BUILD of $597 million, attributed broadly to increased macroeconomic uncertainty (skew toward downside scenario with ~7% unemployment), U.S. Cards seasonal mix and the Barclays American Airlines portfolio forward purchase, and Banking corporate credit reflecting macro uncertainty and exposure growth — not CRE-specific dynamics. Office CRE was not mentioned.
  > "Embedded in the firm-wide net ACL build is a farther skew to the downside scenario, reflecting the increased uncertainty in the macroeconomic outlook. Our reserves now incorporate an eight-quarter weighted average unemployment rate of approximately 5.4%, which continues to include a downside scenario average unemployment rate of nearly 7%." - Gonzalo Luchetti (CFO)
- **USB** [flat] Management did not announce a discrete CRE reserve build or release this quarter; the overall allowance for credit losses held at nearly $8 billion, or 2.0% of period-end loans. CRE was cited as one of the broad-based loan-growth drivers, and the Amazon card book reserve was flagged as a forthcoming build in Q3 at card-book levels, but no specific office-CRE reserve action was discussed.
  > "our allowance for credit losses of nearly $8 billion represented 2% of period-end loans" - John Stern
- **PNC** [flat] PNC signaled CRE has reached an inflection point with balances ticking up ~$100M in Q1 after prolonged runoff, and management expects moderate CRE growth through the rest of 2026. On reserves, the overall allowance held flat at 1.52% of total loans; management referenced past heavy reserving for office CRE as largely complete, with no incremental office-driven reserve build this quarter.
  > "CRE balances reached an inflection point and increased approximately $100 million; we expect moderate growth through the remainder of the year." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [flat] CRE nonperforming loans actually improved quarter over quarter, and no explicit reserve build or release for CRE was announced. Management did provide expanded disclosure on the NDFI portfolio (12% of total loans), emphasizing structural protections and conservative underwriting, but made no specific statement about building or releasing CRE-specific loan-loss reserves this quarter.
  > "Nonperforming loans held for investment increased 2 basis points linked quarter to 50 basis points of total loans, driven by higher consumer and nonperforming loans, partially offset by improvement in C&I and CRE." - Michael Maguire (CFO)

### Loan growth outlook
*DIVERGENCE -- accelerating (4), steady (2), soft (1). Odd ones out on 'soft': JPM*

- **JPM** [soft] Organic loan growth expectations are modest across most of the franchise: Card loans targeted at ~6%+ growth (organic, the most significant driver); Home Lending faces headwinds from First Republic portfolio roll-off (acquisition-related drag); Markets-driven wholesale loan growth (data-center lending, secured financing) is opportunistic. Balance sheet growth this quarter was primarily in Markets (low-risk-density, secured financing) and is largely seasonal, not a new trend. Management will walk away from loans if credit terms are unsatisfactory.
  > "In terms of the firm-wide overall outlook, I think, arguably the single most significant number is the what we said about Card loan growth expectations at Company Update, which is that we said we expected 6% or maybe a little bit more. and that hasn't really changed... In the rest of the franchise, it's really pretty modest growth overall. We actually have some headwinds in Home Lending as a result of some First Republic portfolio roll-off and stuff like that." - Chief Financial Officer — Jeremy Barnum ⚠️UNVERIFIED
- **BAC** [accelerating] Average loans grew ~9% YoY, driven primarily by commercial portfolios (organic client demand and revolver utilization recovery); consumer loans grew ~4% YoY including 3% credit card growth and strong securities-based lending. All growth was explicitly described as organic — no acquisitions were referenced as a driver.
  > "Average balances grew nearly 9% year-over-year driven primarily by client demand in our commercial portfolios. That growth was broad-based, and it reflects good core operating client activity." - Alastair Borthwick, CFO
- **WFC** [accelerating] Loan growth was entirely organic; period-end loans rose 11% YoY surpassing $1 trillion for the first time since 2020, driven by commercial & industrial and consumer portfolios (ex-mortgage). Management raised the possibility that full-year average loan growth could exceed the prior mid-single-digit assumption if demand persists, noting revolver utilization has not yet increased—meaning additional upside is possible from core commercial clients.
  > "Period-end loan balances grew 11% from a year ago and exceeded 1 trillion dollars for the first time since 2020. Average loans increased 87.8 billion dollars, or 10%, from a year ago, driven by growth in commercial and industrial loans as well as growth across our consumer portfolios, except for residential mortgage." - Michael Santomassimo (CFO)
- **C** [steady] Loan growth is entirely organic. Management was emphatic — 'we are only interested in and focused on organic growth. Period.' — and explicitly rejected any acquisition speculation. Net end-of-period loans grew 1% sequentially and ~5% ex-Markets YoY, driven by export agency finance and working capital in Services, securities-based lending in Wealth, and financing activity in Markets. U.S. Cards loans were up 4% YoY but declined seasonally in the quarter. The Barclays American Airlines co-branded card portfolio is an upcoming acquisition that is already embedded in full-year NII guidance.
  > "we are only interested in and focused on organic growth. Period." - Jane Fraser (CEO)
- **USB** [accelerating] Average loans grew 3.8% YoY (5.3% ex-2025 loan sales), driven organically by credit cards, C&I (food & beverage, energy, healthcare), and CRE. Management revised its full-year loan-growth view upward from ~3%-4% to 'probably more in the mid-single-digit range.' Growth is entirely organic; the BTIG acquisition adds fee revenue, not loans, and was explicitly excluded from guidance.
  > "We have talked about loan growth being in that 3% to 4% range, but I certainly think it is going to be higher than that — probably more in the mid-single-digit range — for the full year." - John Stern
- **PNC** [accelerating] Average loans grew $23B (7%) QoQ to $351B; on a spot basis, $15B came from FirstBank and $14B was organic legacy PNC growth — described as a three-year high in organic loan growth. Full-year 2026 average loan growth guidance was raised to ~11% YoY from an earlier ~8%, though management cautioned that H2 visibility is lower. Growth was broad-based in C&I, driven by new production, higher utilization, and expansion market momentum.
  > "On a spot basis, loans increased $29 billion, or 9% from year-end, including $15 billion from the FirstBank acquisition and $14 billion of growth in legacy The PNC Financial Services Group, Inc. loans." - Rob Reilly, CFO
- **TFC** [steady] Loan growth was entirely organic; M&A was explicitly ruled out as a priority. Average loans held for investment grew $2.3B or 0.7% QoQ to $327B, driven by 1.8% commercial loan growth, partially offset by a 0.9% consumer decline. Full-year 2026 average loan growth guidance is maintained at 3%-4%, weighted toward commercial and specialty consumer (Sheffield, LightStream, Service Finance), with indirect auto and residential mortgage intentionally de-emphasized for return reasons.
  > "Based on our current pipeline and economic outlook, we continue to expect average loan growth of approximately 3% to 4% in 2026." - Michael Maguire (CFO)

### Macro / consumer-health view
*DIVERGENCE -- cautious (4), balanced (2), constructive (1). Odd ones out on 'constructive': BAC*

- **JPM** [cautious] Management described the consumer and small business as currently resilient—spend growth above last year's pace, healthy credit metrics, supported by a strong labor market and higher tax refunds—but struck a distinctly cautious tone on the cycle outlook, warning that a credit downturn will likely be worse than the market expects, with asset prices falling and credit spreads widening. Geopolitical risk (Middle East) was flagged as a potential catalyst for deal and economic disruption.
  > "if there's a credit cycle, I do expect it will be worse than people think relative to the scenario. It's not a disaster. We're used to the credit cycles. We'll be big boys about it. But asset prices will go down, credit spreads will going down. People may get a little nervous about some of those things." - Chairman & Chief Executive Officer — James Dimon
- **BAC** [constructive] Management struck a constructive tone: BAC's research team projects ~2% U.S. GDP growth, elevated inflation through 2026–2027, and moderate global growth. Consumer spending through BAC channels remained up 5% YoY into early Q1 2026 with debit/credit card spend up 6%. Management acknowledged tariff, geopolitical, and trade risks but said impacts have been 'measured and absorbed,' and described the macro backdrop as 'constructive.'
  > "In our view, while those risks are out there, the macro backdrop remains constructive and a diversified business model position us well to continue to deliver for you across a range of economic scenarios." - Brian Moynihan, CEO
- **WFC** [cautious] Management struck a cautious-but-not-alarmed tone: the labor market is cooling orderly, consumers remain resilient in aggregate but are increasingly bifurcated by income, and higher energy prices are expected to weigh on lower-income households and broader spending in H2 2026. Commercial clients are described as approaching the remainder of the year cautiously, and management acknowledged economic impact from current conditions is likely but unpredictable in magnitude.
  > "it is likely there will be some economic impact based on what has already occurred, but there are both risks and potential mitigants, so it is hard to predict the ultimate impact." - Charles Scharf (CEO)
- **C** [cautious] Management struck a cautious tone, noting that Middle East conflict is weighing harder on Asia and Europe than on the U.S. and Brazil, that inflation is a rising risk, and that central banks may lean toward more restrictive policy. The reserve model was skewed further toward a downside scenario (7% unemployment). CEO Fraser cautioned against extrapolating Q1 strength, citing an 'unclear macro environment ahead.'
  > "the impact of the Middle East conflict is hitting Asia and Europe harder than countries such as the U.S. and Brazil, which are more insulated from energy shocks. Clearly, the longer this goes on, the more pronounced the second- or third-order impacts are going to be around the world. And inflation is now a greater risk to growth and will likely cause central banks to lean towards more restrictive monetary policies." - Jane Fraser (CEO)
- **USB** [balanced] Management described the macro backdrop as 'constructive despite some softening of sentiment,' pointing to stable consumer spend, solid loan pipelines, and improving credit delinquency trends. Tariff uncertainty was acknowledged as creating some rate-path ambiguity, but corporate clients were said to be pivoting from AI/M&A-driven activity toward core middle-market CapEx expansion, which management views as a healthy demand signal.

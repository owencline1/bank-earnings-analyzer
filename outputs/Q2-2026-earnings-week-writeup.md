## The quarter in one paragraph

It was a beat-and-raise quarter for six of the seven. Citigroup printed its best revenue in a decade ($24.8B, +14%), USB a record $7.7B with the largest guide-raise of the group (FY revenue 4–6% → 7–9%), WFC grew EPS 25%, BAC pushed NII guidance to the top of its range, JPM raised its NII guide ~$1.5B, and PNC guided FY NII up 15%+. Credit improved essentially everywhere, nobody built commercial real estate reserves, and five of seven increased capital return. The exception — and the story of the quarter — is Truist.

---

## Divergence 1 — NII outlook: six up, Truist down (again)

**The split:** JPM, BAC, PNC raised guidance; WFC, C, USB printed expanding NII; **TFC cut its full-year NII growth guide a second time, +2–3% → +1–1.5%** — after already cutting from +3–4% in April.

**Why it isn't a rate-view disagreement anymore.** In April TFC's cut was about "higher for longer." This time management named three causes: deliberate runoff of low-return consumer books (marine/RV exited, auto production down ~40% — this *raises* ROTCE while lowering NII), loan-spread compression of 5–10 bps, and deposit mix shifting out of noninterest-bearing accounts (DDA expected ~27% → ~25% by year-end — their words: the single largest headwind). Peers face the same spread environment and raised anyway, because their deposit franchises are outperforming: JPM's raise was driven primarily by deposit balances and mix, BAC has grown noninterest-bearing deposits seven straight quarters.

**My read:** this is a **franchise divergence, not a macro divergence**. TFC isn't wrong about its own balance sheet — the optimization is real and ROTCE went from 13.8% to ~15% — but the same week showed the gap between a deposit base that funds an NII raise and one that leaks into rate-seeking products. The counterweight: TFC delivered the promised $1.2B buyback (on a $5B FY target, >100% of earnings returned) with CET1 still rising to 10.9%. Cheapest-multiple bank buying back the most stock relative to size, with a new CEO (Mike Lyons) incoming — the M&A-target narrative from Q1 didn't die; Mike Mayo just reframed it on the call as a decade of "dead money."

**Hidden divergence worth flagging:** the guides embed opposite Fed paths. BAC's NII guide assumes a **25 bp hike in September**; PNC's base case is the Fed **on hold all of 2026**. Two raised guides, contradictory rate assumptions — one of them is getting a tailwind they didn't model.

## Divergence 2 — CRE reserves: the feared build never came

**The split:** BAC, WFC, PNC, TFC all **released** CRE reserves; USB flat (its flagged $160M Q3 build is Amazon-portfolio acquisition accounting, not credit stress); JPM and C didn't discuss CRE at all.

Going into the week the live question was "who builds CRE reserves." The answer on live data: **nobody**. The releases are evidenced, not vibes — PNC explicitly said it has "worked through" its office book and released as it resolved credits; TFC's release was driven by resolved problem credits and improving office/multifamily NPLs; BAC's criticized commercial exposures fell $2.3B on CRE improvement; WFC's allowance shifted from CRE to card/auto growth.

**My read:** the releasers are right on this quarter's evidence — resolved credits and falling nonaccruals justify releases. The honest risk statement: releasing is procyclical. Four banks are drawing down the cushion at the same moment, into a strong tape. If the 2027 refi wall reprices office paper harder than expected, this quarter is where the cushion left. Watch item, not an objection.

## Divergence 3 — Deposit costs: a genuine three-way split

**The split:** falling (BAC, WFC — mix-driven), peaking (JPM, PNC — costs drifting but modeled), rising (USB, TFC — rate-seeking migration), C mixed/stable.

**My read:** this dimension is the quarter's cleanest **funding-quality ranking**, because everyone faces the same rate environment and the outcomes differ by franchise. BAC's costs fall because operating accounts keep growing; PNC's rate paid actually fell 5 bps and management guides a drift back up "not dramatic"; USB's rise is loan-growth-conditional; TFC's is structural mix erosion feeding directly into its NII cut. Same weather, different roofs.

## The near-consensus (say these fast)

- **Credit:** improving at 5 of 7, stable-at-strong at BAC, normalizing at C. JPM *cut* its card loss guide to ~3.2% on better-than-expected consumer performance. NCO standouts: WFC 0.34%, PNC 25 bps. The consumer is fine in every book.
- **Capital return:** five increasing (C: +12% dividend and a $30B buyback plan; PNC: +18% dividend; WFC: +11% dividend, $7B H1 buybacks; TFC >100% payout; USB deferred-but-committed) vs JPM/BAC steady. The intellectual bookend: Dimon argues buybacks at ~3x tangible book are a bad investment; TFC is buying back the group's cheapest stock at maximum pace. Both positions are internally consistent — price discipline, applied from opposite ends.
- **Macro:** constructive (BAC — raised GDP forecast to 2.2%, consumer spend +6%; USB; PNC) vs balanced (JPM — "close to as good as it gets… don't know how long it lasts"; WFC; C; TFC). Nobody bearish.

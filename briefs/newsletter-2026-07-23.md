# Weekly Bank Coverage Brief — week of 2026-07-23

## The throughline

The defining story this week is the bifurcation of the deposit franchise. Banks with strong, sticky, low-cost deposit bases — JPM, BAC, WFC, and PNC — are raising or holding NII guidance and watching deposit costs peak or fall. Banks with rate-sensitive books and deposit mix pressure — TFC and USB — are either cutting guidance or flagging that costs will rise alongside loan growth. The market is enforcing that distinction sharply: JPM popped on a record profit and raised guidance, while TFC is the week's worst performer (-4.81%) despite a 37% EPS beat, punished for a NII guide cut driven by deposit remix and spread compression. Credit quality is broadly improving across all seven names, and CRE reserves are releasing at five of seven — those are now table stakes, not differentiators. The real conversation has moved to NII duration: who can sustain it into H2 and 2027, and who is managing it away in exchange for ROTCE. Dimon's rejection of buybacks at 3x tangible book and Demchak's dismissal of NIM as a relevant metric both signal that management teams are running out of easy wins and redefining the goalposts.

## JPMorgan Chase (JPM)

**Market vs. narrative:** Alignment. The market rewarded the NII raise and record profit cleanly (+1.25%). The latent divergence is on capital — management is signaling preference for organic or inorganic deployment over buybacks at these levels, but the stock's multiple assumes continued aggressive capital returns. If Dimon follows through and pulls back on repurchases in favor of M&A or infrastructure, the return-of-capital premium gets tested.

JPMorgan delivered record Q2 profits and raised its full-year NII guide to approximately $96.5B ex-markets (~$105.5B total). The driver was deposit balance strength and favorable consumer deposit betas, not rate movement. Card NCO guidance was cut to approximately 3.2%, reflecting better-than-expected consumer credit performance across FICO cohorts. CET1 sits at 14.1%, and Dimon explicitly rejected buybacks at roughly 3x tangible book as the right use of capital — he pointed instead to infrastructure, AI, Europe, and organic growth. The stock's +1.25% move reflects clean alignment between results and narrative on current earnings, but the capital deployment question is building beneath the surface.

**Key points:**
- NII ex-markets guide raised from ~$95B to ~$96.5B; deposit balance growth and consumer deposit beta improvement, not rate, drove the raise
- Card NCO rate guide cut to ~3.2%; consumer credit performing better than expected across cohorts
- Dimon rejected buybacks at ~3x TBV; excess capital being evaluated for infrastructure, AI, Europe, and organic deployment
- Stock +1.25% week-over-week; pushed into IBD buy zone post-earnings; capital deployment divergence building below the surface

## Bank of America (BAC)

**Market vs. narrative:** Mild divergence. Management raised NII guidance to the upper end of 6-8%, but the clarifying exchange with analyst Mike Mayo revealed that 8% is likely a ceiling, not a base case floor. The stock fell 1.02% despite a strong quarter — the market is looking through the beat to the H2 comp challenge.

Bank of America posted NII of $16.2B (+9% YoY) and raised full-year NII guidance, with loan growth accelerating for a ninth consecutive quarter. The structural funding advantage is real and well-documented: seven consecutive quarters of non-interest-bearing deposit growth (+4% YoY), consumer deposit rate paid just 48bp on a $957B deposit base. The Mayo exchange was the tell — investors had reportedly already told him the guide was conservative, so management's "upper end of 6-8%" clarification landed as a ceiling rather than a floor. The stock's 1.02% decline reflects the market looking past H1 execution toward the harder H2 comps. Management's posture is deliberate sandbagging; the question is whether the setup allows for upside or merely limits downside.

**Key points:**
- NII $16.2B, +9% YoY; full-year guide raised to upper end of 6-8%; one 25bp September rate hike embedded
- Seven consecutive quarters of NIB deposit growth; consumer deposit rate 48bp on $957B — structural funding cost advantage intact
- Loan growth ninth consecutive quarter of acceleration; average loans $1.2T, +8% YoY, entirely organic
- Stock -1.02% despite the beat; Mayo exchange revealed the guide as a ceiling; H2 comps are the watch item

## Wells Fargo (WFC)

**Market vs. narrative:** Divergence. The written-release-only dynamic is suppressing narrative conviction. Loans, NII, credit, buybacks, and deposit costs all improved; the stock is down 2.49% and trading 14% below Daiwa's fresh $100 price target.

Wells Fargo is delivering the structural re-rating thesis in real time: average loans up 12% YoY, NII up 5% YoY, dividends rising 11%, $7B in buybacks in H1 — all enabled by removal of the asset cap constraint. Deposit costs are falling and acting as a direct NII tailwind across segments, including WIM where NII rose 17% YoY. NCO rate dropped sharply from 0.45% to 0.34% QoQ, and CRE reserves are releasing. The paradox is the stock: down 2.49% despite Daiwa raising its target to $100 and maintaining Outperform. The most likely explanation is the absence of a live earnings call transcript — WFC reported via 8-K only, meaning the market lacks the Q&A texture that builds conviction. The fundamentals are not in dispute; the narrative layer simply isn't there.

**Key points:**
- Average loans +12% YoY, +3% QoQ to $1,026.5B; CIB loans +26% YoY; first full cycle of unconstrained post-asset-cap growth
- NCO rate 0.34% annualized, down from 0.45% QoQ and 0.44% YoY; CRE reserves releasing
- Deposit costs falling; lower deposit pricing cited as primary NII driver across multiple segments including WIM (+17% NII YoY)
- No live call transcript — 8-K release only; stock -2.49% despite Daiwa PT raise to $100; narrative vacuum is the divergence

## Citigroup (C)

**Market vs. narrative:** Alignment with a reservation. The strong print, $30B buyback, and decade-high revenues are broadly priced in — the flat stock signals the market is not disbelieving. The reservation is USCC credit: losses are rising nominally even as provisions fall, and the American Airlines card portfolio onboarding adds portfolio risk the market is watching before adding conviction.

Citigroup posted revenues at a decade high ($24.8B), EPS of $3.15, and NII up 13% YoY across all five business segments. Capital return is firmly underway: 92% payout ratio in Q2, a $30B buyback launched, and a 12% dividend increase. The transformation narrative — preferred redemption, digital efficiency, five-segment simplification — is gaining traction without a live call transcript to drive it, which reflects how well-established the story has become. Net credit losses rose 8% YoY to $2.4B, but provision fell 12% YoY and the ACL/funded loans ratio is declining. CEO Jane Fraser's characterization of credit performance as "better than expected" signals improving internal confidence. The American Airlines card portfolio onboarding is the near-term wildcard for credit normalization.

**Key points:**
- Revenue $24.8B, EPS $3.15; NII +13% YoY across all five segments — decade-high revenue level
- 92% payout ratio in Q2; $30B buyback launched; 12% dividend increase — capital return story firmly established
- Net credit losses $2.4B (+8% YoY); provision -12% YoY; ACL/funded loans ratio declining; USCC credit normalizing
- Stock -0.14% — market neutral; monitoring USCC credit normalization and American Airlines card onboarding before adding conviction

## U.S. Bancorp (USB)

**Market vs. narrative:** Divergence. The earnings story is unambiguously strong and the analyst upgrades are real — but a Vice Chair selling $2.3M in stock after a 40% run is the market's read-through signal that near-term appreciation may be limited. USB's management is guiding higher; USB's insiders are trimming. The stock is pricing the middle ground.

U.S. Bancorp posted record revenue of $7.7B (+10.1% YoY), ROTCE of 18.7%, and NII up 7.5% YoY, triggering back-to-back upgrades from J.P. Morgan and Evercore ISI. The forward setup looks constructive: NII guidance north of 5%, a path to 3% NIM in 2027, loan growth raised above mid-single-digit, and the Amazon Small Business portfolio (~$1.6B) arriving mid-August — which will trigger a $160M reserve build but is additive to loan growth. Deposit costs are rising modestly (2 bps in Q2), and management tied future pricing directly to loan growth velocity. The Vice Chair's $2.3M sale following a ~40% run is the uncomfortable fact the market can't look past; it reads as an insider signaling fair value is approaching even as analysts are raising targets.

**Key points:**
- Record revenue $7.7B (+10.1% YoY); ROTCE 18.7%; NII +7.5% YoY; upgraded by JPM and Evercore ISI post-print
- Loan growth raised above "mid-single-digit"; Amazon Small Business portfolio (~$1.6B) closing mid-August, $160M reserve build to follow
- Deposit costs up 2 bps in Q2; management flagged further increases if loan growth stays strong
- Vice Chair sold $2.3M in stock after ~40% run — insider sale is the divergence; stock -1.57% even as analysts upgrade

## PNC Financial (PNC)

**Market vs. narrative:** Divergence. Management beat and raised on NII, declared NIM below 3% irrelevant to the investment case, and hiked the dividend 18%. The market disagreed (-2.12%), Deutsche Bank downgraded to Hold, and the NIM threshold remains a psychological line in the sand the market will not let go until PNC crosses it.

PNC posted EPS of $4.81/$4.85 adjusted, NII up 4% sequentially to $4.1B, and raised full-year NII guidance to +15-15.5%. The dividend rose 18% to $2.00/share, CRE office workout is nearing completion with reserve releases underway, and credit quality improved across every metric. NIM reached 2.96% — close to 3%, but not there. CEO Demchak's Q&A exchange made clear management views the NIM focus as the wrong lens: PNC is buying high-quality C&I loans with lower spreads that grow EPS, not chasing yield. The economics are sound, but the market applies a regional bank NIM heuristic, and 2.96% is not 3.00%. Deutsche Bank's downgrade to Hold at a $265 target (vs. ~$248 spot) is the tell: the easy re-rating has happened, and the market will wait for NIM to validate management before expanding the multiple further.

**Key points:**
- NII +4% sequentially to $4.1B; full-year guide +15-15.5%; NIM 2.96% — management explicitly argues NIM is the wrong metric
- 18% dividend hike to $2.00/share; CRE office workout nearing completion; credit quality improved across all metrics
- Deutsche Bank downgraded to Hold, PT $265; stock at ~$248 — market is not disputing earnings trajectory, debating whether the multiple re-rates before NIM crosses 3%
- Demchak Q&A pushback reflects high internal conviction; market skepticism persists until NIM crosses the psychological threshold

## Truist Financial (TFC)

**Market vs. narrative:** Sharp divergence. Management beat EPS by 37%, accelerated buybacks, and showed improving credit across every metric. The stock fell 4.81% because the NII guide cut and deposit mix deterioration signal that the underlying earnings engine is working against structural headwinds the team is managing around rather than through.

Truist beat Q2 estimates convincingly — EPS up 37% YoY — and is returning capital aggressively ($1.2B in buybacks in Q2, $5B full-year target), with CET1 up 10 bps to 10.9% despite it. Yet the stock is the week's worst performer. The market's verdict is the NII guide cut to +1-1.5% from +2-3%, driven by three forces management laid out: deliberate portfolio optimization (marine/RV exit, ~40% cut in auto production), loan spread compression of -5 to -10 bps YoY, and DDA deposit remix from 27% toward approximately 25% as clients chase rate. The logic is coherent. The market reads it as an admission that the deposit franchise is losing the rate-seeking battle. Mike Mayo's "dead money while peers doubled" exchange went without specific forward commitments from management, and incoming CEO Mike Lyons is the stated narrative pivot — but the market needs proof of concept before crediting the ROTCE re-rating story.

**Key points:**
- EPS +37% YoY, beat estimates; NII full-year guide cut to +1-1.5% from +2-3%; DDA deposits remixing from 27% toward ~25% as clients chase rate
- Loan spread compression -5 to -10 bps YoY; marine/RV exited, auto production cut ~40% — portfolio optimization is deliberate but reduces NII near-term
- Buybacks $1.2B in Q2, $5B full-year target; CET1 +10 bps to 10.9% despite aggressive return pace; capital story is strong
- Stock -4.81% week-over-week; Mayo "dead money" exchange unanswered with specific commitments; incoming CEO Lyons is the narrative pivot but market awaits proof

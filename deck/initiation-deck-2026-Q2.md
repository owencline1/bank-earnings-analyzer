## Sector view — what Q2 2026 actually showed

**A beat-and-raise quarter for six of seven.** Records at Citi (best revenue in a decade) and USB (record revenue, largest guide-raise of the group). Credit improved essentially everywhere — JPM *cut* its card-loss guidance on consumer strength. The feared commercial-real-estate reserve build never came: four banks released CRE reserves (BAC, WFC, PNC, TFC), none built. Capital return rose at five of seven.

**Three divergences carry the sector story:**

1. **NII: six up, one down — twice.** TFC's second consecutive guide-cut is a franchise gap, not a rate call: peers raised *because of* deposit strength (JPM's raise driven primarily by deposit balances and mix; BAC's seventh straight quarter of noninterest-bearing growth) while TFC's checking balances leak into higher-rate products. Embedded-assumption alert: BAC's guide assumes a September rate hike; PNC's assumes the Fed on hold all year. One of them gets an unmodeled tailwind.
2. **Deposit costs split three ways** — falling (BAC, WFC), peaking (JPM, PNC), rising (USB, TFC). Same rate environment, so this is the cleanest funding-quality ranking the quarter produced, and it maps directly onto the NII divergence.
3. **Capital philosophy bookends:** JPM declines buybacks at ~3x tangible book and calls buybacks "an investment decision"; TFC returns >100% of earnings at the group's most depressed valuation. Both are price discipline — from opposite ends of the multiple spectrum.

**The risk to the sector read:** four banks released CRE reserves simultaneously into a strong tape. Justified by resolved credits this quarter — but if office repricing bites in 2027, Q2 2026 is the quarter the cushion left.

## JPMorgan Chase (JPM) — Equal-weight

**Thesis.** The franchise is executing at a level the rest of the group can't match — $16.9B of net income, 23% ROTCE ex-items, a CET1 ratio (14.1%) that is itself an argument, and an NII guide raised ~$1.5B on deposit strength. The problem is not the bank; it is that nothing in Q2 suggests the premium to peers widens from here, and management's own capital behavior says the stock is expensive: they would rather deploy excess capital into infrastructure, AI, and Europe than repurchase shares at ~3x tangible book.

**What Q2 changed.** NII guide up (~$96.5B ex-markets, ~$105.5B total); card NCO guide *down* to ~3.2%; expenses +15% — the one soft spot, which management defended unapologetically ("perpetual operating leverage is a crazy notion").

**Top risks.** (1) Expense growth outpacing revenue ex-items; (2) the "as good as it gets" problem — Dimon's own framing — where credit, spend, and markets are all at cycle bests simultaneously; (3) deposit-beta convexity if rates rise meaningfully (management flagged it).

## Bank of America (BAC) — Equal-weight, positive tilt

**Thesis.** The steadiest NII compounder in the group: guidance raised twice this year and now pointed at the top of the 6–8% range, net interest yield on a stated path from 2.08% to ~2.30%, funded by a deposit franchise that has grown noninterest-bearing balances seven consecutive quarters. Credit is "flattened out at very strong historical levels." It lacks a re-rating catalyst — which is why it's not an Overweight — but the earnings floor keeps rising.

**What Q2 changed.** EPS +34%; ninth straight quarter of loan growth (+8% YoY, all organic); criticized commercial exposures down $2.3B on CRE improvement; $8B returned.

**Top risks.** (1) The NII guide embeds a September rate hike — if it doesn't come, the top of the range moves out of reach; (2) rate sensitivity of the long-duration securities book; (3) consumer spend running +6% is a tailwind that normalizes eventually.

## Wells Fargo (WFC) — Overweight

**Thesis.** The first full quarter with the asset cap gone showed exactly what was pent up: average loans +12% YoY with CIB up 26%, EPS +25%, and the group's best credit (0.34% NCOs). The self-help lever is still unpulled — a ~60% efficiency ratio, worst of the big four, is the margin opportunity the others already harvested. Capital return is accelerating ($7B of H1 buybacks, dividend +11%) from a 10.3% CET1 with buffers to spare.

**What Q2 changed.** Growth thesis moved from promise to print; CRE reserves released; NIM compressed to 2.43% but NII still grew 5% on balance-sheet expansion — the model works without rate help.

**Top risks.** (1) NIM is the group's second-lowest and still falling — the growth-over-margin trade has to keep outrunning compression; (2) expense discipline is unproven at this growth rate; (3) coverage caveat: Q2 assessment is release-based (no earnings-call Q&A available for this quarter — see sourcing note), so management pushback went untested.

## Citigroup (C) — Overweight

**Thesis.** The widest gap in the group between delivery and credit given for it. Q2: best quarterly revenue in a decade ($24.8B, +14%), profit +45%, investment banking +44%, every one of the five businesses growing — against a 13.0% ROTCE that is still the group's lowest. That gap is the opportunity: a $30B buyback plan and a 12% dividend increase from a 12.8% CET1 position force the arithmetic in shareholders' favor while the turnaround compounds. If Fraser's traction holds, the returns gap to peers closes from both ends — earnings up, share count down.

**What Q2 changed.** The turnaround stopped being a projection: services record, markets +17%, equities +40%, expenses up only 5% against 14% revenue growth.

**Top risks.** (1) Execution history — Citi turnarounds have broken before, and a 13% ROTCE leaves no cushion for a stumble; (2) outsized international/geopolitical exposure (management's own forward-looking language: elevated inflation, Middle East conflicts); (3) coverage caveat: release-based quarter, no Q&A tested the guide (see sourcing note).

## U.S. Bancorp (USB) — Equal-weight, positive tilt

**Thesis.** The quarter was the group's most improved: record revenue, the largest guide-raise (FY revenue 4–6% → 7–9%), 400 bps of positive operating leverage, and an 18.7% ROTCE second only to JPM's ex-items number. The rating stays Equal-weight because the capital story asks for patience: buybacks are parked at $200M/quarter while capital funds BTIG and the Amazon small-business portfolio, and the promised buyback acceleration is conditioned on reaching ~10% adjusted CET1. The 9.4% AOCI-inclusive CET1 is the number that explains the caution.

**What Q2 changed.** The NII/fee inflection was confirmed, not just claimed; BTIG contributed $98M in its first month; fee mix reached ~44% of revenue.

**Top risks.** (1) BTIG's $200M/quarter run-rate claim is one strong month annualized — capital-markets revenue is volatile; (2) the AOCI capital drag constrains the payout path if rates stay high; (3) the flagged $160M Q3 reserve build (Amazon portfolio) will optically dent an otherwise clean credit trend.

## PNC Financial (PNC) — Equal-weight

**Thesis.** A disciplined beat-and-raise: FY NII guided +15–15.5%, NIM crossing 3% by year-end on mechanical fixed-asset repricing that management says runs "well into '27 and beyond," credit the cleanest in the group (25 bps NCOs, NPLs -10%), and an 18% dividend increase. What holds it at Equal-weight: management itself guided second-half loan growth back to GDP pace, the office-CRE workout is finished as a reserve-release tailwind, and CET1 at 9.9% is the thinnest in the group just as buybacks are guided flat.

**What Q2 changed.** The 3% NIM commitment moved from target to arithmetic; First Bank integration costs ran through expenses ($261M of items) with the core ratio still ~56%.

**Top risks.** (1) Guided H2 deceleration — Q2 is the high-water mark by management's own words; (2) capital thinness limits flexibility if RWA growth accelerates; (3) NIM-focused investor narrative (Demchak explicitly refuses to manage to it) creates recurring expectations friction.

## Truist Financial (TFC) — Underweight

**Thesis.** The only bank of the seven whose earnings engine is guided backward: FY NII growth cut April's +3–4% to +2–3%, and now to +1–1.5% — while every peer raised or grew. The causes are the group's worst: deposit mix eroding (DDA 27% → ~25% expected, named by management as the largest NII headwind), loan spreads compressing, and the softest loan growth (+0.7% LQ) even adjusting for deliberate consumer runoff. The supports are real — $1.2B quarterly buybacks on a $5B/year program (>100% of earnings), CET1 rising to 10.9% anyway, ROTCE up to 15.4%, EPS +37% YoY, credit improving (NCOs 50 bps), and a new CEO (Mike Lyons) arriving with a mandate — but they are supports for the stock, not evidence the franchise is winning share of the industry's revenue pool. Underweight relative to six peers that are.

**What Q2 changed.** The lone-cutter status was confirmed a second time and re-attributed: less about rates, more about the balance sheet. The ROTCE optimization is delivering (13.8% → 15.4%), which is precisely why the equity story is now "shrink profitably" — a valid strategy that still ranks last in a sector-relative framework during an up-cycle.

**Top risks to the Underweight.** (1) Acquisition/interest: the cheapest large deposit franchise with an admitted decade of underperformance is a natural target, and a bid ends the debate overnight; (2) the new-CEO reset could re-base expectations low enough to beat; (3) if the Fed cuts, TFC's liability-sensitive complaints (deposit remix) ease faster than peers' asset-side tailwinds fade.

## Sourcing & method (read before quoting)

- Built entirely from Q2 2026 primary sources: earnings-call transcripts for JPM, BAC, USB, PNC, TFC; SEC 8-K earnings releases (EX-99.1) for all seven; financial supplements where filed. **WFC and C are release-based** — their Q2 earnings-call transcripts were not publicly available at the time of writing, so no management Q&A informs those two sections; this is flagged in each affected thesis.
- Every management quotation referenced in the underlying analysis was verified verbatim against its source document by an automated check (52 quotes verified, zero mismatches; two mis-stitched quotes were caught and corrected before publication).
- Ratings are sector-relative and fundamentals-based. No market prices or valuation multiples were used; valuation references (e.g., JPM at ~3x tangible book) are management's own on-the-record statements.
- Figures marked n/d were not disclosed in the documents used and were left blank rather than estimated.
